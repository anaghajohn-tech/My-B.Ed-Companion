Q.1 Define Educational Research · Importance · Types of Research
2+6+2 Marks
▼
(a) Define Educational Research — 2 Marks
Educational research is a systematic study directed toward gaining knowledge and understanding of the educational process, with the aim of finding solutions to educational problems and improving the quality of education.

It is a systematic effort to acquire new knowledge about all aspects of education. It primarily focuses on improving quality and searching for knowledge in the field of education.

(b) Importance of Educational Research — 6 Marks
  1. Provides solution to all the problems related to education
  2. Helps in the development of an organized body of scientific knowledge about educational issues
  3. Helps in understanding, explaining, predicting and controlling the behavior of the learner
  4. Helps in acquiring information regarding different aspects of education
  5. Helps in curriculum development and reforms, educating slow learners, understanding psychological traits of physically challenged individuals
  6. Helps in adapting methods of instruction to the needs of individual learners
  7. Helps to achieve the goals and objectives of education in a more scientific way
  8. Helps in adopting technology in education to improve quality
  9. Helps improve teaching and learning methods through evidence-based practices
  10. Helps in curriculum design based on research evidence
(c) Types of Research — 2 Marks

There are three main types of research in education:

  • Fundamental Research: Done for the sake of knowledge; develops theories and principles; no immediate practical application
  • Applied Research: Aims to solve an immediate practical problem; adapts theories from fundamental research to real settings
  • Action Research: Conducted by teachers to solve local classroom problems; concerned with improving practice, not generalizing findings
Q.2 What is Action Research? & Steps with Classroom Problem
2+8 Marks
▼
(a) What is Action Research? — 2 Marks
Action research is a form of investigation designed for use by teachers to attempt to solve problems and improve professional practices in their own classrooms. It involves systematic observations and data collection which can be used by the practitioner-researcher in reflection, decision-making and the development of more effective classroom strategies. — Parsons and Brown, 2002

Action research is concerned with a local problem and is conducted within a local setting. The primary purpose is providing solution to the given problem, not contribution to science.

(b) Steps of Action Research — 8 Marks

Example Problem: "Students of Class 7 are unable to solve problems involving fractions in Mathematics."

  1. Identification of the Problem Area
    Teacher notices students consistently failing in fraction problems. The broad problem area is identified: "poor performance in Mathematics."
  2. Pinpointing the Specific Problem
    The specific problem is stated precisely: "Students of Class 7 are unable to solve word problems involving fractions in Mathematics."
  3. Analyzing Probable Causes
    Causes identified: (i) Lack of understanding of fraction concepts, (ii) Insufficient practice, (iii) Only lecture method used, (iv) Students lack prerequisite multiplication skills.
  4. Listing Objectives
    To identify specific errors; to improve performance through visual aids and activity-based learning; to verify whether intervention reduces errors.
  5. Formulating Action Hypotheses
    "If activity-based learning is used while teaching fractions, then students' performance will improve." Written in "if…then" form.
  6. Designing the Action Plan
    (i) Pre-test on fraction problems, (ii) Programme: activity-based lessons using fraction strips and real-life examples for 3 weeks, (iii) Post-test parallel to pre-test.
  7. Implementation of Action Plan
    Execute the programme as scheduled — activity lessons, practice sessions, feedback given after each lesson.
  8. Statistical Analysis and Interpretation
    Error analysis, marks lists prepared, mean calculated for pre and post tests, bar graphs drawn, individual comparisons made, interpretations stated.
  9. Verification of Hypotheses
    If post-test mean is significantly higher, hypothesis is accepted. Conclusions apply only to this specific class.
  10. Reporting
    A systematic report is prepared documenting all steps and findings for future reference and evidence.
Q.3 Measures of Central Tendency & Main Components of Research Report
2+8 Marks
▼
(a) Measures of Central Tendency — 2 Marks

Central tendency refers to a statistical measure that identifies a single value as representative of an entire distribution. The three measures are:

  • Mean: Arithmetic average = Sum of all scores ÷ Number of scores (Σfx/N)
  • Median: The mid-point of a distribution; 50% of scores fall below and 50% above. Formula: LRL + [(N/2 – fc)/fw] × i
  • Mode: The most frequently occurring score in the distribution
(b) Main Components of Research Report Format — 8 Marks

The Action Research Report is written under three main headings:

1. Preliminary Section
  • Cover Page: Title of study, institution, candidate name, date of submission, guided by
  • Preface: Brief statement and scope of the report
  • Acknowledgement: Thanks to people who helped complete the project
  • Table of Contents: Major divisions with page numbers
  • List of Tables and List of Figures
2. Main Section
  • Identification and pin-pointing of the problem
  • Analyzing probable causes (general and specific)
  • Objectives of Action Research
  • Formulation of action hypothesis
  • Designing the Action Plan
  • Execution of Action Plan
  • Data Analysis — marks lists, error analysis, graphical representation, individual and statistical comparison through mean/median
  • Verification of Action Hypothesis — key findings and discussion
  • Conclusion — summary in terms of the problem stated
3. Reference Section
  • Bibliography: List of all sources and materials used throughout the project
  • Appendix: Supporting material — test papers, data sheets etc. A report of 3000–5000 words is appropriate if publishing findings.

An achievement test is a mechanism used in any educational process to assess educational outcomes and evaluate different levels of learners.

Achievement tests measure the extent to which a student has acquired certain knowledge or skills, usually as a result of instruction.

Q.7 Why is Knowledge of Action Research Necessary for a Classroom Teacher?
5 Marks
▼

Knowledge of Action Research is extremely necessary for a classroom teacher for the following reasons:

  • Solves classroom problems scientifically: Teachers face many day-to-day problems (poor attendance, low performance, discipline issues). Action research gives them a scientific method to identify, analyze and solve these problems systematically
  • Improves teaching quality: By trying out new teaching strategies and evaluating their effectiveness through action research, teachers constantly improve their methods
  • Professional development: Action research turns teachers into reflective practitioners who examine their own teaching, leading to continuous professional growth
  • Understanding students better: Through systematic observation and data collection, teachers gain deeper insight into individual student needs, learning styles and difficulties
  • Evidence-based decisions: Instead of making decisions based on intuition alone, the teacher makes decisions based on actual data and evidence collected from the classroom
  • Develops research skills: The teacher gains practical skills in data collection, statistical analysis (mean, median, graphs) and interpretation of results
  • School improvement: When teachers conduct action research and share findings, it contributes to overall school improvement
  • Empowerment: Action research empowers teachers to take ownership of problems in their classroom and find their own solutions rather than waiting for external experts
  • Helps slow learners: By identifying specific difficulties through diagnostic tools, teachers can plan targeted remedial programmes for slow learners
  • Builds confidence: Seeing evidence of their own positive impact on student learning builds teacher confidence and motivation
Q.8 Importance of Observation Schedule and Questionnaire in Action Research
5 Marks
▼
(a) Observation Schedule in Action Research

Meaning: An observation schedule is a form which directs a researcher to pay attention to certain behaviors and situational features when carrying out an observational study.

Importance:

  • Helps the teacher systematically record student behavior in the classroom during and after the action programme
  • Provides direct, first-hand evidence about classroom interactions and student participation
  • Makes observation more focused and objective — the teacher knows exactly what to observe
  • Can be used to track changes in student behavior before and after the intervention
  • Data collected through observation schedules can be quantified and analyzed statistically
(b) Questionnaire in Action Research

Meaning: A questionnaire is a list of questions used to gather data from respondents about their attitudes, experiences, or opinions.

Importance:

  • Helps gather information about students' perceptions and attitudes related to the problem
  • Can identify probable causes of the problem from students' own perspective
  • Can be administered to an entire class simultaneously, saving time
  • Responses are standardized and easy to analyze
  • Useful as both a pre-action tool (to understand extent of the problem) and post-action tool (to assess change in attitudes)
  • Cost-effective and practical for classroom use

Q.3 Define Action Research & Steps with Classroom Problem
2+8 Marks
▼
(a) Define Action Research — 2 Marks
Action research is a form of investigation designed for use by teachers to attempt to solve problems and improve professional practices in their own classrooms. It involves systematic observations and data collection which can be used by the practitioner-researcher in reflection, decision-making and the development of more effective classroom strategies.— Parsons and Brown, 2002
(b) Steps of Action Research — 8 Marks

Example Problem: "Students of Class 8 make frequent spelling errors while writing English."

  1. Identification of the Problem Area
    Teacher notices frequent spelling mistakes in students' written work. Broad area: "poor English writing skills."
  2. Pinpointing the Specific Problem
    Specific problem stated: "Students of Class 8 make frequent spelling errors in English written work, especially in commonly used words."
  3. Analyzing Probable Causes
    Causes: (i) Students do not read regularly, (ii) Poor retention of spelling rules, (iii) No regular dictation practice, (iv) Teacher does not give feedback on written work.
  4. Listing Objectives
    To identify most common spelling errors; to improve spelling through daily word practice and dictation exercises; to verify improvement through pre and post tests.
  5. Formulating Action Hypotheses
    "If daily spelling practice and dictation exercises are given, then the frequency of spelling errors will decrease significantly." Written as "if…then" statements.
  6. Designing the Action Plan
    (i) Pre-test: dictation of 20 commonly misspelled words, (ii) Programme of Action: daily word lists, word walls, dictation exercises over 4 weeks, (iii) Post-test: parallel dictation of 20 words same difficulty level.
  7. Implementation of Action Plan
    Programme executed as scheduled — daily word practice, weekly dictation, individual feedback on errors, word games.
  8. Statistical Analysis of Data
    Error analysis of pre and post tests, marks lists prepared, mean scores calculated for both, bar graphs drawn to show improvement, individual comparisons made.
  9. Verification of Hypotheses
    Hypotheses accepted or rejected based on pre-test vs post-test mean comparison. Conclusions applicable only to this specific class.
  10. Reporting
    A detailed systematic report prepared for future evidence, following the same steps as carried out.

Q.6 Main Components of Action Research Report · Bibliography & Appendix

The Action Research Report is written under three sections: Preliminary Section · Main Section · Reference Section

Main components include: Cover page, Preface, Acknowledgement, Table of Contents, Problem identification, Probable causes, Objectives, Hypotheses, Action Plan, Data Analysis, Verification of hypotheses, Conclusion, Bibliography, and Appendix.

(b) Bibliography and Appendix — 3 Marks

Bibliography: The bibliography is the record of all sources and materials that have been used for the study. It provides a detailed list of all the sources of material used throughout the project. If the number of sources is large, the researcher may divide the bibliography into sections — one for books, one for periodicals and journals, one for websites etc. Each entry typically includes: Author's name, Year of publication, Title of the book/article, Publisher, Place of publication.

Examples include: Test papers (pre-test and post-test), Data analysis sheets, Questionnaires used, Observation schedules, Raw data tables, Photographs. If the researcher intends to publish the findings, a report of 3000–5000 words (excluding the Reference List) is appropriate.

Q.7 Graphical Representation of Data & Steps for Frequency Polygon
2+3 Marks
▼
(a) What is Graphical Representation of Data? — 2 Marks

Graphical representation is a form of visually displaying data through various methods like graphs, diagrams, charts, and plots. It helps in sorting, visualizing, and presenting data in a clear and understandable manner.

In action research, graphical representations such as bar graphs, histograms, and frequency polygons are drawn to visually show the comparison between pre-test and post-test scores, helping in making interpretations about the effectiveness of the action plan.

(b) Steps to Construct a Frequency Polygon — 3 Marks
  1. Step 1
    Choose the class interval and mark the values on the horizontal (X) axis.
  2. Step 2
    Mark the mid-value of each class interval on the horizontal axis (e.g., for CI 10–14, mid-value = 12).
  3. Step 3
    Mark the frequency of each class on the vertical (Y) axis.
  4. Step 4
    Place a point at the intersection of each mid-value and its corresponding frequency.
  5. Step 5
    Join all the plotted points by straight lines to obtain a bell-shaped curve.
  6. Step 6
    Close the polygon by bringing the lines down to the X-axis at both ends. The result is a Frequency Polygon.

Q.2 Meaning of Action Research & Steps with Suitable Problem
2+8 Marks
▼
(a) Meaning of Action Research — 2 Marks
Action research is a form of investigation designed for use by teachers to attempt to solve problems and improve professional practices in their own classrooms. It involves systematic observations and data collection which can be then used by the practitioner-researcher in reflection, decision-making and the development of more effective classroom strategies. — Parsons and Brown, 2002

Action research is designed to reveal operative ways of dealing with problems in the real world. It is concerned with a local problem and is conducted within a local setting. The primary purpose is providing solution to the given problem.

(b) Steps of Action Research — 8 Marks

Example Problem: "Students of Class 8 are weak in solving mathematical word problems."

  1. Identification of the Problem Area
    The first and most important step. The teacher feels self-dissatisfaction and identifies the problem area causing concern in day-to-day classroom activities. For example, the teacher notices that Class 8 students consistently fail in word problems in Mathematics.
  2. Pinpointing the Specific Problem
    A general problem area is usually exhaustive. The teacher selects a specific problem and states it precisely and unambiguously. Example: "Students of Class 8 of XYZ School are unable to solve word problems in Mathematics involving fractions."
  3. Analyzing the Probable Causes of the Problem
    The teacher diagnoses the specific difficulties. Probable causes identified: (i) Students lack reading comprehension skills, (ii) Students have not mastered basic fraction operations, (iii) Teacher uses only lecture method without visual aids. This helps fix the objectives of the study.
  4. Listing the Objectives of the Study
    Objectives are fixed to make the study purposeful and fruitful. Example objectives: (i) To identify the specific errors made by students in solving word problems, (ii) To improve students' performance through visual teaching aids, (iii) To verify whether use of visual aids reduces errors.
  5. Formulating Action Hypotheses
    The teacher restates the problem into specific, testable "if…then" hypotheses. A hypothesis is an intellectual guess about the relationship between variables. Example: "If visual aids are used while teaching word problems, then students' performance will improve." Action hypotheses act as guidelines for further process of action research.
  6. Designing the Action Plan
    This step involves three aspects:
    (i) Preparation of Diagnostic Tool (pre-test): A pre-test paper is prepared based on the probable causes. Tools used: achievement tests, diagnostic tests, checklists, questionnaires.
    (ii) Designing the Programme of Action: A plan is prepared based on the process of inquiry — e.g., teaching using visual aids, step-by-step problem solving demonstrations.
    (iii) Preparation of Post-Test Tool: Parallel to the pre-test, same number of items, same level of difficulty, same objectives tested.
  7. Implementation / Execution of Action Plan
    The whole action plan is scheduled according to learners' needs. A timetable is prepared specifying what to do, how much to do, when to do, how long to do, and when to evaluate. The programme is executed as planned.
  8. Statistical Analysis and Interpretation of Data
    Steps: (i) Error analysis of pretest and posttest papers separately, (ii) Drawing error analysis graph, (iii) Preparing marks list of both tests, (iv) Analysis of individual performance by comparing scores, (v) In case of group performance, mean values in both tests are calculated and compared, (vi) Graphical representations such as histograms, frequency polygon or bar charts are drawn, (vii) Interpretations are made on the basis of analyzed data.
  9. Verification of Action Hypotheses
    The proposed action hypotheses are either accepted or rejected based on the comparisons of pretest and posttest performance. Conclusions are drawn with respect to the verified hypotheses. These conclusions hold good only for the group of learners involved in the action research process.
  10. Reporting
    A systematic and detailed report of the action research undertaken is prepared for future evidence. The format of the report is not very rigid — it follows the same steps as it was carried out.
Q.3 Mean and Median — Compute & Interpret (CI: 10-14 to 40-44, N=50)

(a) Definitions — 3 Marks

Mean: Mean is an arithmetic average. It is obtained by adding all the scores or measures and dividing that by the number of scores.

Mean = Σfx / N

Median: The mid-point in a distribution, below which 50% of scores fall and above which 50% of scores fall.

Median = LRL + [(N/2 – fc) / fw] × i

LRL = Lower Real Limit of median class | fc = Cumulative frequency before median class | fw = Frequency of median class | i = Class width

Part B — Answer Any Three (5 Marks Each)
Q.4 Importance of Action Research in Teaching-Learning Process
5 Marks
▼

Teachers as researcher and students as change-receivers both profit much from action research. Action research provides a framework that guides the energies of teachers toward a better understanding of why, when, and how students become better learners.

  • Teachers investigate their own practice in new ways, looking deeper in what they and their students actually do and fail to do
  • Teachers develop a deeper understanding of students, the teaching-learning process and their role in the education of both teachers and students

5 Marks
▼

A Bar diagram is a graphical representation of a frequency distribution in the form of rectangles with class-interval as basis and corresponding frequencies as heights. It consists of a set of bars drawn vertically or horizontally. The scores are assumed to be spread uniformly over the entire class interval.

Steps Involved in Drawing a Bar Graph
  1. Convert Class Intervals
    Convert the class interval into exclusive form if the given class intervals are inclusive (e.g., convert 10-14 to 9.5-14.5).
  2. Draw Axes
    Draw two straight lines cutting each other at right angle at a point O, the origin. The horizontal line is the X-axis and the vertical line is the Y-axis.
  3. Mark Scale
    Take a suitable scale. Mark the class intervals on the X-axis (starting from the lowest class interval) and the frequencies on the Y-axis.
  4. Construct Rectangles
    Construct rectangles with class intervals as the base and the frequencies as the height. Each rectangle should be adjacent to the next without gaps.

(b) Steps to Construct a Frequency Polygon — 3 Marks
  1. Choose Class Interval

  2. Mark Mid-Values

  3. Mark Frequencies
    Mark the frequency of each class on the vertical (Y) axis.
  4. Plot Points
    Place a point corresponding to each class's mid-value and its frequency.
  5. Join Points
    Join all the plotted points by straight lines to obtain a bell-shaped curve.
  6. Complete the Polygon
    Bring the line down to the X-axis at both ends to close the polygon. The result is a Frequency Polygon.

(c) Types of Research — 4 Marks

1. Fundamental Research — Also known as basic research, focused on generating new knowledge and understanding of fundamental principles. Primarily concerned with advancing theoretical knowledge and developing new concepts, theories and models. Conducted in universities and research institutions using surveys, experiments, case studies.

2. Applied Research — Also known as practical research, focused on solving real-world problems in education. Designed to produce practical and useful knowledge that can be applied in educational settings. Conducted in educational institutions, government agencies, and non-profit organizations.

3. Action Research — Conducted by educators in their own classrooms or educational settings. The aim is to improve teaching and learning outcomes by identifying and implementing effective strategies and practices. Involves a cyclical process of planning, action, observation, and reflection.

Q.3 Questionnaire, Observation Schedule & Interview — Meaning, Types & Importance
2½+2½+2½+2½ Marks
▼
(a) Questionnaire

Meaning: A questionnaire is a list of questions or items used to gather data from respondents about their attitudes, experiences, or opinions. Questionnaires can collect both quantitative and qualitative information.

(b) Observation Schedule

Meaning: An observation schedule is a form which directs a researcher to pay attention to certain behaviors and situational features when carrying out an observational study. It is a structured tool that lists the specific behaviors or events to be observed and recorded.

(c) Interview — Types

Interview is a process of communication or interaction in which the interviewee gives the needed information verbally to the interviewer in a face-to-face or one-to-one situation.

Three types:

  • Structured Interview: Pre-determined questions answered in the same order by all interviewees. Data analysis is straightforward as answers can be compared easily.
  • Unstructured Interview: No questions prepared prior; conducted informally. Least reliable; high level of bias; comparison of answers is difficult.
  • Semi-Structured Interview: Contains elements of both. Same set of questions for all, but additional questions may be asked to clarify or expand issues.
(d) Diagnostic Test

Meaning: A diagnostic test is primarily used to diagnose student difficulties or learning disabilities. It is a form of pre-assessment that allows the teacher to determine students' individual strengths, weaknesses and skills — before or after instruction.

Importance: Evaluates students' academic achievement; helps plan differentiated instruction; allows teachers to plan meaningful and efficient instruction; creates a baseline for assessing future learning; helps provide remediation or enrichment; very useful for a classroom teacher to identify what is known before instruction begins.

Q.4 Graphical Representation — Meaning & Steps for Frequency Polygon
2+3 Marks
▼
(a) Graphical Representation — 2 Marks

Graphical representation is a form of visually displaying data through various methods like graphs, diagrams, charts, and plots. It helps in sorting, visualizing, and presenting data in a clear manner through different types of graphs. Statistics mainly use graphical representation to show data in a way that is easy to understand and interpret.

Types: Histogram, Bar Graph, Frequency Polygon, Pie Chart, Line Graph, Scatter Plot.

(b) Steps to Draw a Frequency Polygon — 3 Marks
  1. Step 1
    Choose the class interval and mark the values on the horizontal (X) axis.
  2. Step 2
    Mark the mid-value of each class interval on the horizontal axis.
  3. Step 3
    Mark the frequency of each class on the vertical (Y) axis.
  4. Step 4
    Place a point corresponding to each class and its frequency (at the intersection of mid-value and frequency).
  5. Step 5
    Join all the plotted points by separate straight lines to obtain a bell-shaped curve.
  6. Step 6
    The obtained representation is a Frequency Polygon. Close the polygon by bringing lines down to the X-axis at both ends.
Q.5 Action Research — Importance for Educators & Professional Development
5 Marks
▼

Action research empowers educators to design personalized development plans. By identifying areas of improvement and setting targeted goals, educators can tailor their professional growth to nurture their strengths and address their weaknesses.

  • Action research focuses on solving specific classroom or school problems, improving practice, and helping to make decisions at a local site
  • Encourages teachers to examine the dynamics of their classroom and challenge existing practices
  • Helps teachers engage in critical reflection — thinking deeply about what works and what does not in their teaching
  • Teachers become researchers — they develop skills of systematic observation, data collection, and evidence-based decision making
  • Helps improve student outcomes by identifying effective strategies through trial and reflection
  • Creates a culture of continuous improvement and professional learning in schools
  • Builds teacher confidence as they see evidence of their own impact on student learning
  • Promotes collaboration with colleagues — action research can be conducted individually or in teams
  • Contributes to school-wide improvement when findings are shared among staff
  • Helps teachers stay current with educational practices and adapt them to their own context
Q.6 Main Components of Research Report Format
5 Marks
▼

The Action Research Report is written under three main headings:

A. Preliminary Section
  • Title Page — Title of study, institution, candidate name, date, guided by
  • Acknowledgement — Thanks to all who helped
  • Table of Contents — Major divisions with page numbers
  • List of Tables (if any)
  • List of Figures (if any)
B. Main Body
  • Identification of the problem and Pin-pointing the problem
  • Analysis of the problem in terms of probable causes
  • Identifying the objectives
  • Formulation of hypotheses
  • Action Plan
  • Collection and Analysis of data (marks list, error analysis, graphical representation, individual and statistical comparison)
  • Conclusion / Findings — in relation to each hypothesis
C. Reference Section
  • Bibliography — List of all books, journals, periodicals, and websites used
  • Appendix — Test papers, data analysis sheets, questionnaires, and any supporting material not included in the main body
Q.7 Achievement Test — Meaning & Importance
2+3 Marks
▼
(a) Meaning of Achievement Test — 2 Marks

An achievement test is a mechanism used in any educational process to assess educational outcomes and evaluate different levels of learners.

"An achievement or proficiency test is used to ascertain what and how much has been learnt or how well a task has been performed." — Robert L. Ebel
(b) Importance of Achievement Test — 3 Marks
  • Provides systematic analysis of the objectives of the education curriculum; the identification and analysis of objectives is the first step in construction of the achievement test
  • Provides sufficient evidence for decision-making regarding a learner's transition from one level of learning to a higher level
  • Helps in diagnosing strengths and weaknesses in learners' performance, formulating educational plans that ensure development of strengths and treatment of weaknesses
  • Acts as a recognized mechanism for providing indications of each learner's academic development by matching that development to predetermined educational goals
  • Teaches teachers about the effectiveness of modern teaching methods they rely on in the classroom
  • Improves learners' skills and training in dealing with examination pressure
  • Helps identify truth regarding enrollment, retention, dropout, and quality of education
  • Helps build new knowledge regarding methodology, pedagogy, or other core subject areas
Q.8 Problems / Pin-Pointed Problems on which Action Research can be Conducted
5 Marks
▼

The following are common problems identified in classroom and school settings on which action research can be conducted:

  • Problems of spelling errors in languages — Students consistently make spelling mistakes in English or other languages
  • Problem of wrong pronunciation — Students mispronounce words while reading aloud in English
  • Problem of handwriting — Students have poor or illegible handwriting affecting their performance
  • Problem of discipline and code of conduct — Students do not follow classroom rules and show disruptive behavior
  • Problem of poor attendance — Students have irregular attendance affecting their learning
  • Problem of school administration and organization — Inefficient management of school activities and schedules
  • Problems of delinquency — Students showing anti-social or rule-breaking behavior in school
  • Problem of students not participating in classroom activities — Passive learners who do not engage in discussions
  • Problem of low performance in Mathematics — Students performing poorly in specific topics like fractions or geometry
  • Problem of lack of reading habit — Students not developing independent reading skills
Note: Each of these problems can be taken as the starting point for action research by identifying its probable causes, formulating hypotheses, designing a programme of action, and testing whether the intervention reduces or eliminates the problem.
