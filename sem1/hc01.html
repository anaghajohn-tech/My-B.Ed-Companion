Define educational psychology. Briefly explain the scope of educational psychology. How is the knowledge of educational psychology helpful to teachers?

▼
a) Define Educational Psychology (2 marks)
"Educational Psychology is the science of teaching and learning process. It is that branch of psychology which deals with teaching and learning." — Skinner

"Educational psychology is the science of education." — Peel

Educational psychology is a branch of applied psychology that studies how humans learn in educational settings. It focuses on the methods used to teach and the psychological aspects of the learner, the learning process, and the teacher.

b) Scope of Educational Psychology (4 marks)
  • The Learner: Study of the learner's nature — physical, mental, emotional, social growth, individual differences, and special needs.
  • The Learning Process: How learning takes place — theories of learning, memory, forgetting, transfer of learning, motivation, and attention.
  • The Teacher: Role, qualities, mental health, professional development, classroom management skills of the teacher.
  • Teaching Methods & Techniques: Principles of teaching, effective instructional strategies, use of technology.
  • Mental Health & Hygiene: Factors affecting mental health, emotional adjustment of both students and teachers.
  • Measurement & Evaluation: Tests, examinations, psychological measurements to assess learning outcomes.
  • Exceptional Children: Understanding gifted, slow learners, and children with disabilities — guidance and special education.
c) Importance/Usefulness to a Classroom Teacher (4 marks)
  • Understand the Learner: Helps the teacher understand developmental characteristics, individual differences, interests, and needs of students.
  • Effective Teaching: Guides the teacher to select appropriate methods, materials, and strategies that suit the learner's level.
  • Curriculum Planning: Assists in designing age-appropriate, psychologically sound curriculum content.
  • Motivation: Teaches techniques to motivate students, arouse curiosity, and sustain interest in learning.
  • Classroom Management: Helps manage discipline, handle indiscipline, and maintain a conducive classroom environment.
  • Evaluation: Helps the teacher measure learning outcomes accurately and give meaningful feedback.
  • Mental Health: Guides teachers to promote emotional well-being and handle students with anxiety or behavioral issues.
  • Guiding Exceptional Children: Enables teachers to identify and appropriately support gifted or slow-learning students.

What is learning? List out the characteristics of learning. Explain the influence of motivation on learning.

a) What is Learning? (2 marks)
"Learning is the modification of behavior through experience and training." — Gates
"The term learning covers every modification in behavior to meet environmental requirements." — Gardner Murphy

Learning is the relatively permanent change in behavior or knowledge that results from experience, practice, or instruction. It involves acquisition, retention, and modification of knowledge, skills, habits, and attitudes.

b) Characteristics of Learning (4 marks)
  • Purposeful: Learning is goal-directed; it occurs with intention and purpose.
  • Active: The learner must be actively engaged; passive reception is not learning.
  • Product of Experience: Learning results from interaction with the environment through experience.
  • Universal and Continuous: All living beings learn throughout their entire lifespan.
  • Adjustment: Learning helps in intra-personal and inter-personal adjustment with the environment.
  • Maturation-Linked: Learning is related to physical and mental maturation of the individual.
  • Individual and Social: Learning can be personal (individual) or collaborative (social).
  • Affects Conduct: True learning brings about observable behavioral change in the learner.
c) Influence of Motivation on Learning (4 marks)
Motivation is the internal force that drives, directs, and sustains behavior toward a goal.
  • Direction: Motivation directs behavior toward specific learning goals, keeping the learner focused.
  • Increased Effort: Motivated students put in more effort, time, and energy into their studies.
  • Persistence: Motivation sustains engagement even when tasks are difficult, reducing dropouts.
  • Better Cognitive Processing: Motivated learners engage in deeper processing — understanding, not just memorization.
  • Determines Reinforcement: Motivation defines what counts as reward or punishment, shaping habits.
  • Enhances Performance: Motivated students consistently perform better in examinations and tasks.
  • Reduces Anxiety: Intrinsic motivation reduces exam anxiety and promotes self-confidence.

Types: Intrinsic motivation (internal — curiosity, interest) and extrinsic motivation (external — rewards, praise, grades). Teachers should use both effectively — praise, encouragement, meaningful activities, and achievable challenges.

Q.4
What is trial and error learning? Explain Thorndike's experiment. Mention the educational implications.
2+5+3 = 10 marks
▼
a) What is Trial and Error Learning? (2 marks)
"Learning is nothing but stamping in of the correct responses and stamping out of the incorrect responses through trial and error." — Thorndike

Trial and Error (S-R Bond Theory / Connectionism) is a method of learning in which various responses are tentatively tried until a satisfactory solution is found. Incorrect responses are gradually eliminated and the correct response is fixed through repetition.

b) Thorndike's Puzzle Box Experiment (5 marks)

Subject: Hungry cat placed inside a puzzle box. Food (fish) kept outside the box as motivation.

Process:

  • Drive: Hunger was intensified by sight of food outside.
  • Goal: To get out of the box and reach the food.
  • Block: Cat was confined inside with a latch mechanism.
  • Random Movements: Cat scratched, bit, clawed, jumped — made various random movements to escape.
  • Chance Success: By accident, the cat pressed the latch and the door opened.
  • Selection: On repetition, the cat recognized the correct movement (pressing the latch).
  • Fixation: Incorrect responses were eliminated; the correct response was stamped in through S-R bond.

Laws of Learning derived: Law of Readiness, Law of Effect (satisfaction vs annoyance), Law of Exercise (use and disuse).

c) Educational Implications (3 marks)
  • Teacher must wait until the learner is ready — ensure psychological and physiological readiness before new learning.
  • Classroom experiences should be satisfying; avoid creating fear — allow students to experience success.
  • Give ample opportunities for repetition and practice — "Practice makes perfect."
  • Learning should be meaningful and applicable to real life situations.
  • Praise and encouragement reinforce correct responses; criticism should be constructive.
  • Break complex tasks into smaller, achievable sub-tasks to ensure some success at each step.
Q.5
What is insight learning? Explain Kohler's experiment. List the educational implications.
2+5+3 = 10 marks
▼
a) What is Insight Learning? (2 marks)
Insight learning is the abrupt realization of a problem's solution — the sudden perception of relationships among the elements of a problem. It is "Aha! experience."

Insight is a completely cognitive experience involving perception of the whole problem situation at once, not through random trial and error, but through understanding relationships. It is a higher form of learning than trial and error.

b) Kohler's Experiment on Chimpanzee (5 marks)

Subject: Sultan — a chimpanzee kept in a cage.

Setup — Experiment 1 (Stick Problem): A banana was hung out of reach outside the cage. Two sticks (neither long enough alone) were kept inside.

Sultan's Behavior: First tried each stick separately — failed. After resting, Sultan suddenly looked at both sticks, fitted them together, and used the longer combined stick to pull the banana in. — This was the INSIGHT moment (Aha!).

Setup — Experiment 2 (Box Problem): Banana hung from ceiling. Several boxes placed inside.

Sultan's Behavior: After pondering, Sultan stacked the boxes on top of each other and climbed up to reach the banana.

  • Sultan perceived the entire situation as a whole — not in isolated parts.
  • Solution appeared suddenly after a period of contemplation (not gradual).
  • Once achieved, the solution could be repeated in new similar situations — insight transfers!
c) Educational Implications (3 marks)
  • Present subject matter in a Gestalt (whole) form rather than isolated facts — show the big picture first.
  • Minimize rote learning; encourage reasoning, understanding, and problem-solving.
  • Use Gestalt principles in curriculum planning — integrate subjects meaningfully.
  • Motivate learners by arousing curiosity and creating a "discovery" environment.
  • Encourage independent thinking; don't spoon-feed — use heuristic and problem-solving methods.
  • Help students see relevant relationships between concepts across subjects.

Q.6
Explain Albert Bandura's social learning theory.
5 marks
▼
"Behavior is learned from the environment through the process of observational learning." — Bandura (1977)

Social Learning Theory (SLT) states that humans are active information processors who learn by observing others' behavior and its consequences — without direct experience.

Types of Modeling Stimuli
  • Live Model: A real person demonstrating the behavior (teacher, parent, peer).
  • Verbal Instruction: Describing behavior for the observer to follow.
  • Symbolic: Media — TV, movies, books, internet — fictional or real characters.
Four Processes of Observational Learning (ARRM)
  • Attention: Observer must notice and pay attention to the model's behavior.
  • Retention: Observer must remember the behavior in memory (cognitive storage).
  • Reproduction: Observer must have the physical and cognitive ability to reproduce the behavior.
  • Motivation: Observer must be motivated to imitate — anticipation of reward or punishment.

Educational Significance: Teachers should model positive behavior, values, and thinking skills. Students learn by watching their teachers — "Do as I do" is more powerful than "Do as I say."

Q.7
Explain Skinner's experiment on operant conditioning.
5 marks
▼
"Operant conditioning is a method of learning that occurs through rewards and punishments for behavior — reinforcement shapes voluntary behavior." — B.F. Skinner
Skinner's Box Experiment
  • Setup: A hungry rat placed in a box with a lever. When the lever is pressed, food pellet is delivered automatically.
  • Phase 1 — Random Movement: Rat explores the box, presses lever accidentally, food appears. This is the operant.
  • Phase 2 — Reinforcement: Food (positive reinforcement) strengthens the lever-pressing behavior. Rat presses the lever more frequently.
  • Phase 3 — Extinction: When food stops coming, the rat gradually stops pressing the lever.
  • Phase 4 — Discrimination: Rat learns to press lever only when a light is on (stimulus control).
Key Concepts
  • Positive Reinforcement: Adding a pleasant stimulus to increase behavior (praise, reward).
  • Negative Reinforcement: Removing an unpleasant stimulus to increase behavior (removing punishment).
  • Punishment: Adding unpleasant stimulus / removing pleasant stimulus to decrease behavior.
Educational Implications
  • Use praise, rewards, and positive feedback to reinforce good behavior and academic performance.
  • Curriculum should move from simple to complex in small steps — programmed instruction.
  • Immediate reinforcement is more effective than delayed feedback.
Q.8
What is transfer of learning? Explain the types of transfer of learning.
1+4 = 5 marks
▼
"Transfer of learning is the application of skills, knowledge, and attitudes learned in one situation to another." — Transfer refers to carrying over knowledge from one learning situation to another.
Types of Transfer
  • Positive Transfer: Learning in one situation helps learning in another. E.g., Learning piano helps in learning the keyboard. Sanskrit helps in learning Hindi grammar.
  • Negative Transfer: Learning in one situation interferes with learning in another. E.g., Learning to drive on the left side interferes when driving on the right side. Learning Kannada pronunciation may interfere with English pronunciation.
  • Zero/Neutral Transfer: Learning in one situation has no effect on learning in another. E.g., Learning grammar has no effect on handwriting.
  • Bilateral Transfer: A skill learned with one hand can be transferred to the other hand (within the same individual). Also called cross education.

Theories of Transfer: Theory of Identical Elements (Thorndike) — transfer occurs when elements are similar; Theory of Generalization (Judd) — transfer through understanding of general principles.

Thinking skills are the mental processes used to solve problems, make decisions, form concepts, plan, reason logically, and evaluate information creatively.
Types of Thinking Skills
  • Convergent thinking (focused, analytical)
  • Divergent thinking (creative, multiple solutions)
  • Critical thinking (evaluation, judgement)

How to Develop Thinking Skills Among Students
  • Use open-ended questions — "Why?", "What if?", "How?" — that demand reasoning, not recall.
  • Apply problem-based learning — real-world problems where students must reason and find solutions.
  • Encourage Socratic dialogue — guided questioning that pushes deeper thinking.

  • Teach metacognition — self-monitoring and reflection on one's own thinking process.
  • Create a safe, non-threatening classroom where students feel free to voice ideas.
  • Use Bloom's Taxonomy — plan activities at higher levels: Analysis, Evaluation, Synthesis/Creation.
  • Show models of good thinking — demonstrate how to analyze, compare, classify, and conclude.
Q.10
What is remembering? How will you help your students to remember better?
1+4 = 5 marks
▼
Remembering (Memory) is the cognitive process of encoding, storing, and retrieving previously learned information or experiences.
Types of Memory
  • Sensory Memory: Brief retention of sensory input (fraction of a second).
  • Short-Term Memory (STM): Holds limited information for a short time (about 20 seconds).
  • Long-Term Memory (LTM): Permanent storage — can hold unlimited information indefinitely.
Helping Students Remember Better
  • Meaningful Learning: Teach concepts with real-life examples and connections to prior knowledge.
  • Spaced Repetition: Review material at increasing intervals — avoids the forgetting curve.
  • Mnemonics: Acronyms, rhymes, and memory tricks make retention easier.
  • Active Recall: Self-testing and retrieval practice is far more effective than passive re-reading.
  • Multi-sensory Learning: Engage visual, auditory, and kinesthetic channels — charts, models, recordings.
  • Minimize Interference: Avoid teaching similar, confusing material back-to-back.
  • Emotional Connection: Content linked to emotions is remembered more vividly.
  • Adequate Sleep: Encourage proper sleep — memory consolidation happens during sleep.
Q.11
Explain in brief the revised Bloom's taxonomy of educational objectives.
5 marks
▼
Bloom's Taxonomy (revised by Krathwohl et al., 2001) classifies educational objectives into three domains: Cognitive, Affective, and Psychomotor. The revised Cognitive Domain (by Anderson & Krathwohl) uses verbs:
Revised Cognitive Domain (Lower to Higher Order)
  • 1. Remember: Recall facts — Define, List, Name, State. (e.g., Name the parts of a cell.)
  • 2. Understand: Explain in own words — Describe, Summarize, Classify, Explain. (e.g., Describe photosynthesis.)
  • 3. Apply: Use knowledge in new situations — Solve, Use, Demonstrate. (e.g., Solve a math problem.)
  • 4. Analyze: Break into parts, find relationships — Compare, Differentiate, Examine. (e.g., Compare mitosis and meiosis.)
  • 5. Evaluate: Make judgments using criteria — Justify, Critique, Assess. (e.g., Judge the effectiveness of a policy.)
  • 6. Create: Produce new and original work — Design, Construct, Compose. (e.g., Design an experiment.)

The key change from the original: Synthesis was replaced by Create and moved to the top, and Evaluation moved to fifth level. These represent Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) — the goal of quality education.

Q.15
Explain the emotional development of a child with special reference to diverse social and economic background.
5 marks
▼
Emotional Development in Childhood

Infants initially experience only two basic emotions: pleasure and distress. By 9 months, children experience all basic emotions. Emotional development proceeds from simple to complex.

  • Positive Emotions: Social smile (6 weeks), laughter (3–4 months), joy, love, happiness develop progressively.
  • Negative Emotions: Anger, fear (stranger anxiety at 8–9 months), separation anxiety, sadness, jealousy develop as the child grows.
  • Complex Emotions: Embarrassment, shame, guilt, pride develop around 18 months–3 years as self-awareness emerges.
Impact of Diverse Social & Economic Background
  • Economically Disadvantaged: Chronic stress, poverty, food insecurity → emotional dysregulation, anxiety, lower self-esteem, aggression.
  • Economically Privileged: May face emotional detachment from over-busy parents; pressure of expectations → anxiety, depression.
  • Social Background: Children from neglectful or abusive homes show delayed emotional development, trust issues, and attachment disorders.
  • Cultural Diversity: Different cultures have different norms about emotional expression — some suppress emotions (stoic cultures), some are expressive.
  • Teacher's Role: Create emotionally safe classrooms, use empathy, allow emotional expression, and be sensitive to diverse backgrounds.

Q.17
What is maturation? What are the educational implications of maturation?
2+3 = 5 marks
▼
"Maturation is the process of becoming mature — the unfolding of innately determined patterns of growth and development that occur as a result of biological processes, not experience." Maturation is growth from within.

Maturation involves qualitative biological changes in the organism that increase competency and adaptability — independent of practice or experience. E.g., a child can walk only when the muscles and nervous system have matured sufficiently.

Educational Implications of Maturation
  • Readiness: Maturation creates the capacity to learn — teaching before readiness wastes time and creates frustration.
  • Timing: Effective learning depends on proper timing — the teacher must wait for the right stage of maturation.
  • Individual Differences: Children mature at different rates — individualized teaching is essential.
  • Curriculum Design: Curriculum should be planned according to maturational stages — what is appropriate at each age.
  • Learned vs Maturational Behavior: Teachers must distinguish between behaviors that can be taught and those that await maturation.
  • Avoid Pressure: Forcing learning before maturation leads to negative experiences and school phobia.

a) What is Childhood? (1 mark)
Childhood is the age span from birth to adolescence — typically from birth to 12–13 years. It refers to the years of youth before becoming a teenager. Childhood is NOT a uniform period — it includes infancy, early childhood, and late childhood.
b) Intellectual Development of Childhood (3 marks)

According to Piaget, intellectual development in childhood passes through:

  • Sensorimotor Stage (0–2 yrs): Child understands world through senses and motor actions. Object permanence develops.
  • Pre-operational Stage (2–7 yrs): Language develops rapidly. Thinking is egocentric, symbolic, and pre-logical. Cannot conserve.
  • Concrete Operational Stage (7–12 yrs): Logical thinking about concrete objects. Can conserve, classify, and seriate. Decentration and reversibility develop.
  • Vocabulary expands rapidly; reading, writing, and numerical skills develop.
  • Memory, attention span, and concentration improve significantly.
  • Curiosity and questioning are hallmarks — the "why" stage.
c) Emotional Development of Childhood (3 marks)
  • Early childhood emotions are intense, brief, and directly linked to stimuli (tantrum, joy).
  • Late childhood shows increasing emotional control and social referencing.
  • Fear (of animals, dark, strangers), anger (frustration), love (for parents, pets), jealousy (siblings) are common emotions.
  • Empathy and guilt emerge as the child develops moral reasoning.
  • Emotional stability increases as children learn to manage emotions through social learning.
d) Educational Implications (3 marks)
  • Curriculum should use concrete materials — manipulatives, models, charts — before abstract concepts.
  • Learning activities should involve play, exploration, and discovery — especially in early childhood.
  • Teachers must recognize individual differences in cognitive development.
  • Emotional climate of the classroom must be safe, warm, and encouraging for emotional expression.
  • Avoid harsh punishment — it damages emotional development and self-concept.
  • Storytelling, art, music, and drama help children process emotions and develop creativity.

Adolescence is the transitional phase from childhood to adulthood (12–18 years). It involves rapid physical, emotional, social, and cognitive changes. Stanley Hall called it "a period of storm and strife."

Adolescents face the challenge of identity formation (Erikson's Identity vs. Role Confusion stage) — defining who they are in terms of values, beliefs, and goals. They are legally minors but psychologically seeking adult autonomy.

  • 5. Abstract Thinking: Piaget's formal operational stage — ability to think hypothetically, reason about possibilities, and engage in idealistic thinking about society and justice.
  • 6. Desire for Independence: Strong desire to make their own decisions and reduce dependence on parents — conflicts often arise from this tension.

b) Factors Influencing Learning (8 marks)
Factors Related to the Learner
  • Maturation & Readiness: Learning is efficient only when the learner has reached the required maturational level. Premature teaching fails.

Factors Related to the Environment

  • Classroom Environment: Orderly, emotionally safe, resource-rich classrooms enhance learning.
  • Teacher's Quality: Teaching methods, personality, enthusiasm, and feedback quality directly affect student learning.
  • Peer Influence: Cooperative peers facilitate learning; competitive or disruptive peers hinder it.
Factors Related to the Learning Material
  • Meaningfulness: Meaningful, well-organized content is learned and retained faster.
  • Difficulty Level: Material too easy causes boredom; too difficult causes frustration. Optimal challenge (Zone of Proximal Development) is best.

Childhood refers to the developmental period from birth to the onset of adolescence (approximately birth to 12 years). It is subdivided into: Infancy (0–2), Early Childhood (2–6), and Late Childhood (6–12). Childhood is a time of rapid all-round development.
b) Physical Characteristics of Childhood (3 marks)
Early Childhood (2–6 years)
  • Growth slows compared to infancy — average gain: 6–8 cm height, 1.5–3 kg weight per year.
  • Brain reaches 90% of adult size by age 5; myelination continues rapidly.
  • Gross motor skills develop (running, jumping, climbing); fine motor skills (drawing, buttoning) follow.
  • Baby teeth fall; permanent teeth begin appearing.
Late Childhood (6–12 years)
  • Steady, slower growth — "latency period" of physical development.
  • Increased muscular strength and coordination — children excel at sports and physical activities.
  • Pre-pubescent changes begin toward end (ages 10–12): girls begin puberty earlier than boys.
  • Children are energetic and physically active — cannot sit still for long.
c) Intellectual Characteristics (3 marks)
  • Pre-operational (2–7): Symbolic thinking (language), egocentrism, centration, cannot conserve, animistic thinking.
  • Concrete Operational (7–12): Logical reasoning about concrete objects, conservation, classification, seriation, decentration, and reversibility.
  • Rapid vocabulary growth; reading, writing, arithmetic skills develop.
  • Memory, attention, and problem-solving abilities improve steadily.
  • Curiosity-driven, love of exploration and "why" questions.
d) Educational Implications (3 marks)
  • Concrete before Abstract: Use objects, models, and experiments before symbols and formulas.
  • Activity-Based Learning: Incorporate movement, play, and hands-on activities — children learn by doing.
  • Short Activity Spans: Vary activities frequently — young children cannot sustain attention for long.
  • Readiness: Do not teach concepts before the child has reached the required cognitive stage.
  • Discovery Learning: Allow exploration and questioning — do not give answers before children have thought.
  • Physical Education: Regular physical activity is essential — it supports both physical development and cognitive function.

LAW
Thorndike's Three Laws of Learning — Quick Summary
Always include these in Trial & Error answers
▼
  • Law of Readiness: When a conduction unit is ready to act, acting is satisfying. When not ready, acting is annoying. Teaching must wait for learner readiness.
  • Law of Effect: Responses followed by a satisfying state are strengthened; responses followed by annoyance are weakened. Pleasure strengthens bonds; pain weakens them. (Basis of reinforcement.)
  • Law of Exercise (Use & Disuse): A bond strengthened by use; weakened by disuse. Practice and repetition are essential. "Practice makes perfect."
Later Thorndike added: Law of Multiple Response, Law of Set/Attitude, Law of Associative Shifting, Law of Analogy — but the main three are most important for exams.

Q.15
Explain Thorndike's laws of learning.
5 marks
▼
Thorndike (Connectionism / S-R Bond Theory) formulated three primary laws governing learning through his Puzzle Box experiments.
1. Law of Readiness
  • When a conduction unit (animal/learner) is ready to act, acting is satisfying.
  • When not ready, acting is annoying.
  • When ready but forced not to act — that too is annoying.
  • Teaching must wait for the learner's physical, psychological, and intellectual readiness.
2. Law of Effect
  • When a modifiable connection between situation and response is made and is accompanied by a satisfying state, that connection's strength is increased.
  • When made and accompanied by an annoying state, its strength is decreased.
  • Simply: Pleasure (satisfaction) strengthens S-R bonds; Pain (annoyance) weakens them.
  • Rewards and punishments; nothing succeeds like success.
3. Law of Exercise (Use and Disuse)
  • Law of Use: A modifiable connection made between a situation and response — when that connection is used — the connection's strength is increased.
  • Law of Disuse: A connection not used for a length of time decreases in strength.
  • Connections between stimulus and response are strengthened as they are used (practice).
  • Connections are weakened when practice is discontinued — "Use it or lose it."

Secondary Laws: Law of Multiple Response, Law of Set/Attitude, Law of Associative Shifting, Law of Analogy. These elaborate on how learning transfers and adapts.

a) Skinner's Experiment — Official Key (7 marks)
The animal is placed in a situation in which it learns to make a response that brings about the satisfaction of a need. Hence the use of the word "operant" — the subject operates upon or modifies the environment. Operant conditioning refers to a kind of learning where by a response is made more probable or more frequent by reinforcement.

Skinner identified three types of responses (operants) that can follow behavior:

  • Neutral operants: Responses from the environment that neither increase nor decrease the probability of a behavior being repeated.
  • Re-inforcers: Responses from the environment that increase the probability of a behavior being repeated. Re-inforcers can be either positive (adding something pleasant) or negative (removing something unpleasant).
  • Punishers: Response from the environment that decrease the likelihood of a behavior being repeated. Punishment weakens behavior.

BF Skinner coined the term operant conditioning — it means roughly changing of behavior by the use of reinforcement which is given after the desired response. Response occurs in the environment. Reward/reinforcer is not possible unless the response is emitted. Connection's strength between R-S is not increased, but the possibility of occurrence of the same Response is increased.

7+3 = 10 marks · Official Key Available
▼
a) Bandura's Social Learning Theory — Official Key (7 marks)
Albert Bandura (1977) states that behavior is learned from the environment through the process of observational learning. Bandura believes that humans are active information processors and think about the relationship between their behavior and its consequences. Observational learning cannot occur unless cognitive processes were at work.

Social learning theory draws heavily on the concept of modeling — learning by observing a behavior. Bandura outlined three types of modeling stimuli:

  • 1. Live model: An actual person demonstrating the desired behavior.
  • 2. Verbal instruction: An individual describes the desired behavior in detail and instructs the participant in how to engage in the behavior.
  • 3. Symbolic: Modeling occurs by means of the media — movies, television, internet, literature, radio. Stimuli can be real or fictional characters.

Bandura hypothesized a four-step pattern (ARRM) that combined cognitive and operant view of learning:

  • 1. Attention: Notices something in the environment — observer must attend to the modeled behavior.
  • 2. Retention: Remembers what was noticed — stored in memory for future use.
  • 3. Reproduction: Produces an action that is a copy of what was noticed — must have physical/cognitive capability.
  • 4. Motivation: Consequence changes the probability that the behavior will be emitted again — anticipation of reward.
b) Educational Implications — Official Key (3 marks)
  • Observational learning helps in increasing knowledge of learners, retention, and life skills.
  • Teacher should model positive behaviors on himself/herself — "Do as I do."
  • Teacher should expose students to a variety of other models — breaks down traditional stereotypes.
  • Vicarious reinforcement and modeling can be used by teachers to increase and focus learner's attention.
  • Students often learn a great deal simply by observing other people.
  • Describing consequences of behavior can effectively increase appropriate behaviors and decrease inappropriate ones.
  • Teacher and parents must model appropriate behaviors and take care not to model inappropriate behaviors.
  • Students must believe that they are capable of accomplishing school tasks — develop a sense of self-efficacy.
  • Teacher should help students to set realistic expectations for their academic accomplishments.
  • Self-regulation techniques provide an effective method for improving student behavior.

Suggestions to Draw Attention of Pupils in the Classroom — Official Key
  • Nature of stimulus — use stimuli appropriate to the learner's interest and age.
  • Use colour pictures vs B&W pictures to capture maximum attention.
  • Intensity and size of the stimulus — large, bright objects capture more attention.
  • Loud noise, bright colours/light, perfume, large objects — sensory variety attracts attention.
  • Contrast, change, and variety in teaching methods and materials.
  • Contrast makes forceful — use surprising, unexpected content strategically.
  • Newness attracts — introduce novel elements, current events, or puzzles.
  • Repetition of stimulus secures attention — but has diminishing results if used too much.
  • Movement of the stimulus — moving stimuli vs static ones attract more attention.
  • Create novelty and familiarity — balance between something new and something known.
  • Break routine — use illustrations and examples in the lesson; frequent use of AVA.
  • Relate lessons to actual life situations — relevance captures attention.
