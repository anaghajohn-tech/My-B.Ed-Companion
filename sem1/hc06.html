1 a) Mention the aims of LAC approach. b) What are the advantages of considering this approach in the school context? (4+6) 10M ▼
LAC (Language Across the Curriculum) recognizes language as the primary medium of learning in all subjects.

a) Aims of LAC

  • To recognize language as a tool for thinking and learning across all disciplines
  • To make all teachers aware that they are also language teachers
  • To improve students' ability to use language for reading, writing, speaking and listening in subject areas
  • To bridge the gap between language learning and content learning
  • To promote language proficiency as a means of academic success
  • To help students express subject-specific knowledge using appropriate language
  • To encourage collaborative learning through discussion and writing

b) Advantages in school context

  • Integrated learning: Language and content are learnt simultaneously, saving time and effort
  • Improved comprehension: Students understand textbooks and classroom instructions better
  • Active participation: Students engage more when language is natural and purposeful
  • Critical thinking: Students articulate ideas, argue, and reason through language
  • Writing skills: Note-making, report writing, and essay skills develop across subjects
  • Multilingual benefit: Recognizes and uses students' home language as a resource
  • Teacher development: Encourages subject teachers to become language-conscious
  • Better assessment: Students can express what they know more accurately
  • Lifelong literacy: Students develop reading and writing habits beyond the classroom
2 Explain the different strategies that can be adopted to develop writing skills among children. (10) 10M ▼
Writing is a complex cognitive skill. Multiple strategies help children develop it progressively.

1. Process Writing Approach

Writing is taught as a process: pre-writing → drafting → revising → editing → publishing. Students learn that good writing requires multiple drafts, not just one attempt.

2. Guided Writing

Teacher provides a model text, discusses its structure, and then students write a similar text with scaffolding. Reduces anxiety and builds confidence.

3. Free Writing / Journal Writing

Children write freely without fear of correction. Encourages fluency, personal voice, and self-expression. Builds habit of writing regularly.

4. Story Writing

Using story starters, picture prompts, or story frames. Develops narrative skills, imagination, sequencing, and descriptive language.

5. Paragraph and Sentence Construction

Teaching topic sentences, supporting details, and concluding sentences. Students practice linking ideas coherently within and across paragraphs.

6. Note-Making

Students learn to take notes from textbooks, lectures, and discussions. Develops summarizing and organizational skills essential for academic writing.

7. Persuasive Writing

Students write arguments for or against a topic, learning to state opinions, provide evidence, and address counterarguments.

8. Collaborative / Pair Writing

Students co-write, discuss, and improve each other's work. Peer feedback develops critical awareness of language use.

9. Writing across subjects

Subject teachers assign writing tasks (lab reports, geography field notes, math word problems). Reinforces content learning through writing.

3 a) What is communication? b) What are the characteristics of good communication? c) Why do you think it is important in the classroom teaching? (2+4+4) 10M ▼

a) Communication — Definition

Communication is the process of transmitting ideas, thoughts, feelings, and information from one person to another through verbal, non-verbal, or written means, resulting in shared understanding.

b) Characteristics of Good Communication

  • Clarity: Message is expressed in simple, unambiguous language
  • Completeness: Contains all necessary information; nothing important is left out
  • Conciseness: Brief and to the point; avoids unnecessary repetition
  • Correctness: Factually accurate with proper language use
  • Courtesy: Respectful and considerate of the receiver's perspective
  • Consistency: Message is logically organized and free from contradictions
  • Active listening: Good communication is two-way — the receiver listens attentively
  • Feedback: Receiver responds, confirming that the message was understood

c) Importance in classroom teaching

  • Clear teacher communication ensures students understand lessons accurately
  • Encourages student participation, questions, and discussion
  • Non-verbal cues (gestures, eye contact) maintain attention and convey enthusiasm
  • Helps teacher assess student understanding through feedback and responses
  • Creates a positive classroom climate based on mutual respect
  • Models good language use for students to emulate
  • Effective communication of instructions reduces confusion and classroom management problems

4 Explain the nature of transactional text and reflective text with examples. 5M ▼

Transactional Text

Text written to communicate information, instructions, or ideas to others. The focus is on the reader and achieving a practical outcome.

Examples: Letters, notices, reports, advertisements, instructions, business emails, news articles.

Features: Clear purpose, audience-focused, formal/informal register, factual content.

Reflective Text

Text written to explore one's own thoughts, feelings, and experiences. The focus is on the writer's inner world and personal growth.

Examples: Personal diaries, journals, autobiographies, personal essays, reflective portfolios.

Features: First-person narrative, subjective, exploratory, uses descriptive and expressive language.

5 a) What are speech defects? b) Explain how a teacher can resolve any two speech defects. (2+3) 5M ▼

a) Speech Defects

Speech defects are disorders or impairments in the normal production of speech sounds that affect clear communication. They may be caused by physical, neurological, psychological, or environmental factors.

Types: Stuttering (stammering), Lisping, Cluttering, Articulation disorders, Voice disorders.

b) Teacher's role in correcting two defects

1. Stuttering: The teacher should create a patient, non-pressurized environment. Allow the child sufficient time to speak without interruption. Use slow, deliberate speech as a model. Encourage the child through drama and role-play activities.

2. Lisping (mispronunciation of 's' as 'th'): The teacher can use tongue exercises and pronunciation drills. Refer the child to a speech therapist if needed. Use mirror exercises where the child watches tongue placement while speaking.

7 Explain the different functions of language. 5M ▼

Functions of Language (Halliday's model and others)

  • Instrumental: Used to satisfy needs and desires ("I want…")
  • Regulatory: Used to control behaviour of others ("Don't do that…")
  • Interactional: Used to maintain social relationships and interact with others
  • Personal: Used to express personal identity, feelings, and individuality
  • Heuristic: Used to explore and learn about the world ("Why…?", "How…?")
  • Imaginative: Used to create fictional and imaginative worlds (stories, poems)
  • Representational/Informative: Used to convey facts, information, and ideas
  • Emotive/Expressive: Used to express emotions and attitudes
  • Phatic: Used in social/ritual exchanges ("How are you?") — maintains rapport

2 a) Explain Language Acquisition Theory according to Chomsky. b) What are the implementations of Chomsky's language acquisition theory? (6+4) 10M ▼

a) Chomsky's Language Acquisition Theory (LAT / Innateness Theory)

Noam Chomsky proposed that human beings are born with an innate capacity for language acquisition. This is called the Language Acquisition Device (LAD).

  • LAD: A hypothetical mental organ present in the brain that enables children to acquire language naturally
  • Universal Grammar: The LAD contains knowledge of universal grammatical principles shared by all human languages
  • Innate capacity: Children do not just imitate; they generate novel sentences — proof of innate grammatical rules
  • Language acquisition is natural: All normal children acquire language by age 5–6, regardless of intelligence or teaching
  • Critical period: Language acquisition is most effective during early childhood (approximately 2–12 years)
  • Poverty of stimulus: Children acquire complex grammar despite receiving limited and imperfect input — proves innate knowledge

b) Educational Implications

  • Language learning should start early — expose children to rich language environments from birth
  • Avoid over-correction; children will naturally internalize grammatical rules
  • Provide meaningful input; children learn by interacting, not just drilling
  • Second language teaching is best during the critical period (early schooling)
  • Encourage students to generate language creatively, not just memorize sentences
  • Trust the child's natural language-learning capacity; the role of teacher is to provide environment, not impose rigid rules
3 a) Mention the reading strategies. b) Explain the importance of developing reading skill among children. c) Explain skimming and scanning strategies with suitable examples. (2+4+4) 10M ▼

a) Reading Strategies

  • Skimming — reading for gist/overview
  • Scanning — searching for specific information
  • Intensive reading — close, detailed reading for full comprehension
  • Extensive reading — reading widely for pleasure and general understanding
  • Oral reading — reading aloud
  • Silent reading — reading independently without vocalizing
  • Predictive reading — using context clues to anticipate meaning

b) Importance of reading skill development

  • Foundation for all academic learning — students who read well perform better across all subjects
  • Expands vocabulary, general knowledge, and cognitive ability
  • Develops critical thinking and the ability to evaluate information
  • Builds empathy through exposure to different perspectives and experiences
  • Enhances writing skills — reading good texts models good writing
  • Prepares students for lifelong learning in an information-rich world

c) Skimming and Scanning

Skimming is reading quickly to get the general idea (gist) of a text. You read headings, first sentences of paragraphs, and the conclusion.
Example: A student quickly reads through a newspaper article to find out what it is generally about before deciding to read it fully.

Scanning is moving your eyes quickly over a text to find specific information — a name, date, or fact.
Example: Looking through a history chapter to find the year a war ended, or checking a phone book for a specific name.

2 a) What are speech defects? b) What are the causes of speech defects? c) Explain the role of teacher in correcting the speech defects in children. (2+4+4) 10M ▼

a) Speech Defects

Speech defects are abnormalities in the production of speech sounds that deviate from normal speech patterns, making communication difficult.

Types: Stuttering, Lisping, Cluttering, Mutism, Voice disorders, Articulation disorders.

b) Causes of Speech Defects

  • Physical causes: Cleft palate, lip, tongue-tie, hearing impairment
  • Neurological causes: Brain damage, cerebral palsy, stroke
  • Psychological causes: Anxiety, trauma, emotional distress, fear
  • Environmental causes: Lack of language stimulation, bilingual confusion, imitation of poor speech models
  • Developmental causes: Delayed language development due to premature birth, illness, neglect

c) Role of teacher

  • Create a supportive, non-ridicule classroom environment
  • Give the child time to speak without rushing or interrupting
  • Provide a good speech model — speak clearly and correctly
  • Use drama, songs, rhymes, and role-play to build confidence
  • Refer the child to a speech therapist when the problem is serious
  • Coordinate with parents to ensure practice continues at home
  • Avoid drawing public attention to the child's speech difficulty

7 Explain the theory of Language development according to Chomsky. 5M ▼

Chomsky's Theory (Brief)

Chomsky proposed the Innateness Theory / Nativist Theory of language acquisition. Key concepts:

  • LAD (Language Acquisition Device): An innate mental mechanism in humans that processes language input and produces grammar
  • Universal Grammar: All human languages share deep structural rules; the LAD knows these rules innately
  • Acquisition vs. Learning: Children acquire language naturally; they don't simply learn by imitation
  • Critical Period: Best language acquisition occurs in early childhood (before puberty)
  • Children generate novel sentences never heard before — proves rule-governed creativity, not mere imitation

Chomsky's Theory (Innateness/Nativist)

Children have innate capacity for language acquisition. LAD — master language in short time — abstract rules — sufficient vocabulary — consistent utterances. LAD = universal grammar = storehouse of rules — applied in all languages — decipher relationships in any language.

6 What is the nature of expository text? Analyse the expository text. 5M ▼

Nature of Expository Text

Expository text is written primarily to inform, explain, describe, or instruct. It is factual, objective, and organized logically to convey information clearly to the reader.

  • Matter is informative — explains and describes facts and concepts
  • Articles are concise and simple — avoids unnecessary embellishment
  • Information presented with clarity and logical organization
  • Writing is to the point — no fictional or narrative elements
  • Makes true, deliberate points with supporting evidence
  • Uses headings, subheadings, diagrams, and glossaries

Analysis

Examples include textbooks, encyclopaedias, scientific reports, news articles, and instructional manuals. The reader expects clear, verifiable information. The writer avoids personal bias (unlike reflective text) and focuses on the needs of the reader (unlike narrative text).

8 Explain the Scanning and Skimming strategy with an example. (2½+2½) 5M ▼
