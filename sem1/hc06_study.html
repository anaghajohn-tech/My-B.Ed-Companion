Unit 1 · Most Important

Language Across the Curriculum (LAC)

This topic appears in EVERY paper. Learn it thoroughly.

🔥
Exam Frequency

LAC (need / purpose / benefits) appeared in all 9 papers. This is your guaranteed Part A question. Marks: 10.

What is LAC?
Definition

Language Across the Curriculum (LAC) is an educational approach that recognizes language — reading, writing, speaking, and listening — as the primary medium of learning in all subjects, not just in language classes. Every teacher is also a language teacher.

The idea came from the Bullock Report (UK, 1975) which famously said: "Every teacher is a teacher of language." This means a science teacher, a maths teacher, a social science teacher — all of them teach through language and must be aware of that.

Think of it this way: even if you know the concept of photosynthesis, you need language to read about it, write about it, and explain it. Without language, you cannot access any subject.

Need for LAC

Why do we need this approach? Three simple reasons:

  • Subjects depend on language — You cannot read a history book, solve a maths word problem, or understand a science experiment without language.
  • Language and learning are interrelated — You learn by talking, reading, writing, and listening. These are language activities. Separate them and learning breaks down.
  • Content and context lead to effective learning — When language is taught within a real subject context (like writing a lab report in science), it is more meaningful and effective.
💡 Easy to Remember

Need = S-L-C: Subjects depend on language · Language and learning interrelated · Content + context = effective learning

Purpose of LAC
  • Improves language proficiency — Students become better language users across all contexts, not just in English class.
  • Enhances students' learning outcomes — When language skills improve, performance in all subjects improves.
  • Language education leads to learning of specific subjects — Good reading skills help in history; good writing helps in science reports.
  • Promotes learning of the entire curriculum — Language becomes the thread that connects all subjects together.
Benefits of LAC ★ (Official Scheme)
  • More effective teaching and learning — Both teacher and student benefit when language is used purposefully.
  • Awareness of language skill effectiveness — Teachers recognize that their subject has its own language demands.
  • Accelerator to thinking — Language fuels thinking. When students articulate ideas, they think more deeply.
  • Leads to cognitive development — Language development and cognitive development go hand in hand.
  • Increases knowledge and understanding — Reading, writing, and discussion deepen understanding of subject matter.
  • Contributes to support for learning issues — Students who struggle with content often struggle with language; LAC addresses this.
  • Bridges home language and school language — Especially important in multilingual India.
Aims of LAC
  • Recognize language as a tool for thinking and learning in all disciplines
  • Make all teachers aware that they are also language teachers
  • Improve students' reading, writing, speaking, and listening in every subject
  • Bridge the gap between language learning and content learning
  • Promote language proficiency as a means of academic success
Advantages in School Context (2024 paper)

Integrated Learning

Language and content are learnt together — saves time, builds both simultaneously.

Better Comprehension

Students understand textbooks and instructions better when language skills are developed.

Critical Thinking

Students learn to argue, reason, and evaluate through language across subjects.

Teacher Development

Subject teachers become language-conscious; improves quality of teaching.

Multilingual Benefit

Recognizes home language as a resource, especially valuable in India.

Lifelong Literacy

Students develop reading and writing habits that go beyond formal schooling.

Role of Teachers in LAC

Subject Teachers

  • Teach subject-specific vocabulary explicitly
  • Use skimming/scanning when teaching textbooks
  • Assign subject-related writing tasks
  • Create opportunities for discussion and debate
  • Recognize that every lesson is a language lesson

Language Teachers

  • Teach generic literacy skills (reading, writing)
  • Help students with text structures and registers
  • Collaborate with subject teachers
  • Support multilingual learners
  • Provide cross-subject reading material

Functions of Language

Appeared in 6/9 papers. Know all 9 functions with examples.

What are Functions?
Definition

Functions of language refer to the different purposes or uses for which language is employed in everyday life — from expressing needs to creating stories to maintaining social relationships.

The most famous classification is by Michael Halliday, who studied how children use language. He identified 7 functions initially, which are extended to 9 in educational contexts.

The 9 Functions of Language

1. Instrumental

Used to satisfy needs and get things done.
"I want water." "Give me that."

2. Regulatory

Used to control behavior of others.
"Sit down." "Don't run."

3. Interactional

Used to maintain social relationships.
"How are you?" "Nice to meet you."

4. Personal

Used to express personal identity, feelings, individuality.
"I feel sad." "I love poetry."

5. Heuristic

Used to explore and learn about the world.
"Why does this happen?" "How does it work?"

6. Imaginative

Used to create fictional and imaginative worlds.
Stories, poems, plays, myths.

7. Informative / Representational

Used to convey facts and information.
News, lectures, textbooks.

8. Emotive / Expressive

Used to express emotions and attitudes.
"That's wonderful!" "I'm terrified."

9. Phatic

Social ritual — maintains rapport, not to exchange info.
"Good morning!" "How's the weather?"

Functions Inside and Outside Classroom

Inside Classroom

  • Informative — teacher explains content
  • Heuristic — students ask questions
  • Regulatory — teacher gives instructions
  • Interactional — group discussions
  • Personal — students express opinions
  • Imaginative — creative writing tasks

Outside Classroom

  • Phatic — social greetings
  • Personal — expressing identity
  • Instrumental — getting daily needs
  • Regulatory — giving directions
  • Imaginative — storytelling
  • Emotive — expressing feelings

Theories of Language Acquisition

Chomsky appears in 7/9 papers. Chomsky + Skinner together in many papers.

🔥
Exam Tip

Questions ask for: (a) Chomsky's theory alone, or (b) Chomsky + Skinner together, or (c) Chomsky's theory + educational implications. Learn all three parts.

Chomsky's Theory — Innateness / Nativist Theory
Noam Chomsky Nativist / Innateness Theory

Core idea: Human beings are born with an innate (built-in) capacity for language acquisition. Language is not just learned — it is a biological endowment unique to humans.

LAD — Language Acquisition Device: Chomsky proposed that the human brain contains a hypothetical mental organ called the Language Acquisition Device. This LAD "knows" the rules of all human languages in a general form — called Universal Grammar.

Universal Grammar: All human languages share deep structural principles. The LAD stores these universal rules, which is why children can acquire any language they are exposed to.

Evidence — Novel Sentences: Children regularly produce sentences they have never heard before (e.g., "I goed to school"). This proves they are not just imitating — they have internalized grammatical rules and apply them creatively. This is called rule-governed creativity.

Critical Period: Language acquisition is most natural and effective during a critical period — approximately birth to puberty (2–12 years). After this period, acquiring a first language becomes extremely difficult.

Poverty of Stimulus: Children acquire complex grammar even though the language input they receive is imperfect and incomplete. This "poverty of stimulus" is proof that grammatical knowledge must be innate, not learned purely from input.

Educational Implications of Chomsky's Theory ★
  • Start language learning early — Expose children to rich language from birth; take advantage of the critical period.
  • Avoid over-correction — Children will naturally internalize grammatical rules. Constant correction disrupts natural acquisition.
  • Provide meaningful input, not drills — Children learn language by interacting meaningfully, not by memorizing grammar rules in isolation.
  • Second language in early schooling — Use the critical period advantage to introduce second/third languages early.
  • Encourage creative language use — Let students generate their own sentences and express their own ideas. Trust their natural capacity.
  • Create language-rich environments — Storytelling, drama, discussion, read-alouds — these provide the natural input the LAD needs.
Skinner's Theory — Behaviourist Theory ★ (Official Scheme)
B.F. Skinner Behaviourist Theory

Core idea: Language behavior is acquired through operant conditioning — the same way any behavior is learned through reinforcement and punishment.

How it works: When a child produces a correct utterance (e.g., says "Mama"), adults respond positively (smile, repeat, reward). This reinforcement encourages the child to repeat the behavior. Incorrect utterances are not reinforced (or are corrected), so they die out.

Shaping: Language is gradually shaped through reinforcement. Each small correct step is reinforced, leading progressively to more complex language.

Modeling: Continuous modeling of language by adults (parents, teachers) builds vocabulary and syntax over time.

Key difference from Chomsky: Skinner says language is learned (from environment); Chomsky says language is acquired (from innate capacity). Skinner emphasizes nurture; Chomsky emphasizes nature.

Unit 4 · Important

Social Stimulation in Language

Appeared in 5/9 papers (Part A) and 4/9 papers (Part B). Must know.

What is Social Stimulation?
Definition

Social stimulation refers to the social interactions, environmental experiences, and contextual cues that motivate and trigger language development in children. Language is not acquired in isolation — it is acquired through meaningful social engagement.

Vygotsky's idea is central here: language develops through social interaction. The child is surrounded by language users, and through participation in social activities, gradually takes on the language of the community.

Role of Different Social Stimuli ★

1. Family / Home

Parents and siblings are the first and most powerful language models. Richness of conversation, storytelling, and vocabulary at home directly shapes the child's language. A language-rich home = better vocabulary and communication skills.

2. Peers / Play

Interaction with friends develops conversational skills, turn-taking, and negotiation. Children create slang, jokes, and new words through peer interaction. Play is a language laboratory for children.

3. School / Teacher

Introduces formal, standard, academic language. Teachers provide reading, writing, and grammar instruction. The classroom expands the child's linguistic repertoire beyond home language.

4. Community / Neighbourhood

Local cultural events, community language, dialect, and oral traditions shape language identity. The child learns how language works in their specific cultural context.

5. Media (TV, Books, Radio)

Exposes children to varied vocabulary, narrative styles, and diverse language uses. Good quality media — stories, documentaries, news — broadens linguistic range significantly.

6. Religious / Cultural Events

Prayers, rituals, ceremonies, festivals use specialized language forms. These expand the child's register range and cultural-linguistic identity.

Speech Mechanism & Correct Pronunciation ★ (2017 paper)

The physical organs that produce speech are called the speech mechanism or articulatory system.

  • Vowels — Produced by modified laryngeal tones. Voice is produced in the larynx, then shaped in the mouth cavity by the tongue and lips.
  • Consonants — Produced by friction or stoppage of air. The tongue is brought close to the teeth, upper gums, hard palate, or soft palate.
  • Jaw and tongue movement — The mass of muscle tissue in the throat and lower jaw moves upward/backward/downward/forward to produce different sounds.
  • Pronunciation varies — Regional accents and dialects exist because different communities use slightly different articulations of the same sounds.
Unit 5 · Very Important

Study Skills

Appeared in 6/9 papers. Know definition, importance, and how to use for reading.

What are Study Skills?
Definition

Study skills are a set of strategies, methods, and techniques that enable learners to acquire, understand, retain, and apply knowledge efficiently and effectively.

Study skills are sometimes called learning strategies or learning-to-learn skills. They are not automatic — they must be explicitly taught. A student with good study skills can learn independently, without always needing a teacher to guide them.

Types of Study Skills

Reading Skills

Skimming, scanning, intensive reading, extensive reading, SQ3R method.

Note-Making

Organizing information from lectures and texts into structured, summarized notes.

Time Management

Planning study sessions, prioritizing tasks, avoiding procrastination.

Memory Techniques

Mnemonics, mind maps, spaced repetition, association techniques.

Exam Skills

Understanding question formats, answering technique, time management in exams.

Critical Thinking

Evaluating information, forming arguments, analyzing and synthesizing content.

Importance of Study Skills ★
  • Self-directed learning — Students become independent learners who do not always need the teacher to tell them what and how to study.
  • Increased efficiency — Students learn more in less time; they work smarter, not harder.
  • Better retention — Techniques like spaced repetition and mind maps improve long-term memory.
  • Critical thinking — Study skills help students analyze, evaluate, and question information rather than just memorize it.
  • Exam readiness — Organized preparation reduces exam anxiety and improves performance.
  • Lifelong learning — Habits of learning developed in school continue throughout adult life.
Using Study Skills to Develop Reading in Children ★
1
Teach Skimming

Before reading a chapter, skim through headings, bold words, and the first/last sentences. Students get the big picture first, then read for detail.

2
Teach Scanning

For specific information (a date, a name, a fact), train students to move their eyes quickly over the text to locate the target. This builds reading speed.

3
SQ3R Method

Survey (skim) → Question (turn headings into questions) → Read (carefully) → Recite (answer from memory) → Review (check answers). This is a complete reading study strategy.

4
Key Word Identification

Teach students to identify topic sentences and key words in paragraphs. This helps them extract the main idea quickly without reading every word.

5
Note-Making After Reading

After reading a section, students write brief notes or draw a mind map. This consolidates understanding and improves retention.

6
Extensive Reading

Assign storybooks, comics, and newspapers for pleasure reading. Wide reading builds vocabulary, fluency, and love of reading naturally.

Unit 6

Oral Language in the Classroom

Appeared in 4/9 papers. Know importance of oral language and role of discourse.

Importance of Oral Language ★ (Official Scheme)
  • Develops vocabulary — Through oral activities like pictures, games, and flowcharts, students encounter and learn new words naturally.
  • Different reading skills — Oral reading, loud reading, silent reading, intensive, and extensive reading are all forms that oral language supports.
  • Read and draw the message — Oral activities where students read and then represent meaning visually improve comprehension.
  • Articulation and pronunciation practice — Speaking activities develop clear, accurate pronunciation and speech habits.
  • Expression of feelings — Oral language gives students a means to communicate emotions and personal responses.
  • Comprehension of meaning — Listening and speaking activities deepen understanding of concepts.
  • Detect faults and analyze weakness — The teacher can hear students' language difficulties and address them immediately.
Role of Discourse in the Classroom ★ (Official Scheme)
Discourse

Discourse refers to extended, connected language use — conversations, discussions, debates, explanations, and narratives — that goes beyond single sentences. Classroom discourse is the interaction between teacher and students and among students themselves.

  • Teacher-student interactions correlated with student achievement — Research shows that quality of classroom talk directly predicts how much students learn.
  • Acquire ways of thinking and talking — Through disciplinary discourse (how scientists argue, how historians reason), students learn subject-specific thinking.
  • Learn to participate in discussion — Students learn conversational norms: turn-taking, listening, responding, disagreeing respectfully.
  • Higher mental processes through internalization — Vygotsky: social language becomes inner language; discourse drives cognitive development.
  • Knowledge and beliefs are changed — Genuine discussion challenges existing ideas and builds deeper understanding.
  • Structure sentences meaningfully — Participating in classroom discourse teaches students how to organize and present ideas.
Unit 7 · Important

Reading Strategies

Skimming and scanning asked in 5+ papers. Know both with clear examples.

Types of Reading

Oral Reading

Reading aloud. Helps pronunciation, fluency, and confidence. Good for young learners.

Silent Reading

Reading without vocalizing. Faster and used for independent comprehension.

Intensive Reading

Close, careful reading for full comprehension of every detail. Used for difficult texts.

Extensive Reading

Wide reading of easy, interesting texts for pleasure. Builds vocabulary and fluency naturally.

Skimming

Quick reading for the general idea. Read headings, first sentences, and conclusion.

Scanning

Rapid eye movement to find a specific piece of information — a name, date, or fact.

Skimming — Explained ★
Skimming

Reading a text quickly to get the general meaning (gist) without reading every word. You read the title, headings, first sentence of each paragraph, and the conclusion.

When to use it: Before reading a chapter in detail; to decide if an article is worth reading; to preview an exam paper before starting to write.

Example: A student opens a history chapter on World War I. Before reading fully, she quickly reads the heading, subheadings, and the first sentence of each section. In 2 minutes, she knows: the chapter covers causes, major battles, and consequences. Now she reads in detail with this framework in mind.

Classroom use (Social Science): Teacher asks students to skim a newspaper article about elections in 1 minute and report the main topic. This builds pre-reading and comprehension skills.

Scanning — Explained ★
Scanning

Moving eyes rapidly over a text to locate a specific piece of information — a name, number, date, or keyword — without reading the whole text.

When to use it: Finding a specific fact in a textbook; looking up a word in a dictionary; checking a phone number in a directory; finding an answer to a comprehension question.

Example (Mathematics): A student needs to find the formula for the area of a trapezium. She scans the chapter without reading every word, moving her eyes quickly until she spots the word "trapezium." Found in 20 seconds.

Example (Science): A student scans a chapter to find the year penicillin was discovered. She looks for a number that looks like a year rather than reading sentences.

Get an overviewFind a specific fact

Example: Checking if an article is relevantExample: Finding a date or name in a text
SQ3R Method — Full Form
S
Survey

Skim through the chapter — title, headings, pictures, summary. Get the big picture in 2–3 minutes.

Q
Question

Turn each heading into a question. "The Causes of WW1" becomes "What were the causes of WW1?" This gives reading a purpose.

R1
Read

Read each section carefully, looking for answers to your questions. Active, purposeful reading.

R2
Recite

Close the book and recall the answers from memory. Speaking or writing them out reinforces retention.

R3
Review

Go back and check your answers. Re-read anything you could not recall. Final consolidation.

Unit 8 · Important

Writing Strategies

Appeared in 5/9 papers. Know the strategies with brief explanations.

Why Teach Writing Strategies?

Writing is the most complex language skill. Students often struggle because they think good writing means getting it right the first time. Writing strategies teach students that writing is a process, not a single act.

Key Writing Strategies ★
1
Process Writing Approach

Writing is taught as a process: Pre-writing → Drafting → Revising → Editing → Publishing. Students learn that good writing requires multiple drafts. Teachers guide each stage rather than just evaluating the final product.

2
Guided Writing

Teacher shows a model text, discusses its structure, then students write a similar text with teacher support. Reduces anxiety, builds confidence.

3
Journal / Free Writing

Children write freely without fear of correction. Builds fluency, personal voice, and habit of writing. Emphasis is on quantity and expression, not accuracy.

4
Story Writing

Using picture prompts, story starters, or story frames. Develops narrative skills, imagination, sequencing, and descriptive vocabulary. Children who read widely write better stories.

5
Note-Making

Students learn to take notes from textbooks and lectures. Develops summarizing, organizational skills, and the ability to distinguish main ideas from supporting details.

6
Persuasive Writing

Students write arguments for/against a position. Teaches how to state an opinion, provide evidence, address counterarguments, and conclude logically. Develops critical thinking.

7
Paragraph Writing

Teaching topic sentence + supporting sentences + concluding sentence. Students practice building coherent, well-organized paragraphs before writing full essays.

8
Collaborative / Pair Writing

Students co-write, then give feedback to each other. Peer review develops critical awareness of language and teaches revision skills.

9
Writing Across Subjects

Science teachers assign lab reports; geography teachers assign field notes; maths teachers ask students to explain problem-solving steps. Writing reinforces content learning.

Official Scheme Writing Skills ★ (Vijayambika N)
  • Write in simple, correct English in idiomatic language
  • Organize ideas and thoughts in paragraph form
  • Enhance vocabulary of pupils through reading and word study
  • Develop power of imagination through creative writing tasks
  • Develop self-expression with ease through journal and free writing

Transactional vs Reflective Text ★

Transactional Text

Written to communicate with others for a practical purpose. The writer's focus is on the reader's needs.

  • Purpose: to inform, instruct, persuade, or request
  • Audience-focused; formal or informal
  • Examples: letters, notices, reports, news articles, advertisements, emails, instructions

Reflective Text

Written to explore one's own thoughts, feelings, and experiences. The writer's focus is on their own inner world.

  • Purpose: personal reflection, self-exploration, growth
  • First-person; subjective; exploratory
  • Examples: diary entries, journals, autobiographies, personal essays, reflective portfolios
All Four Text Types — Quick Overview

Expository

Informs, explains, describes. Factual, logical. Textbooks, reports, encyclopaedias.

Narrative

Tells a story. Fictional or experiential. Novels, folk tales, myths, autobiographies.

Transactional

Communicates with others for a purpose. Letters, notices, emails, reports.

Reflective

Explores personal inner world. Diary, journal, personal essay, portfolio.

Communication in the Classroom

Good communication characteristics appeared in 7/9 papers.

What is Communication?
Definition

Communication is the process of transmitting ideas, thoughts, feelings, or information from one person to another through verbal, non-verbal, or written means, resulting in shared understanding.

Effective communication is two-way — it requires not just a sender and a message, but a receiver who understands, and feedback that confirms understanding. Communication can be verbal (spoken words), non-verbal (body language, gestures), or written (text, signs).

Characteristics of Good Communication — The 7 Cs ★

1. Clarity

Message expressed in simple, unambiguous language. The receiver should understand exactly what is meant without guessing.

2. Completeness

Contains all necessary information. Nothing important is left out. The receiver should not need to ask follow-up questions for basic details.

3. Conciseness

Brief and to the point. Avoids unnecessary repetition and padding. Respects the receiver's time.

4. Correctness

Factually accurate with proper language use. Errors in facts or grammar undermine credibility and cause confusion.

5. Courtesy

Respectful and considerate of the receiver's perspective and feelings. Does not offend or alienate.

6. Consistency

Logically organized and free from contradictions. Message should be coherent from start to finish.

7. Credibility + Feedback

Trustworthy and honest. Good communication also includes a feedback mechanism — the receiver confirms understanding.

Importance of Communication in Classroom Teaching ★
  • Ensures accurate understanding — Clear teacher communication means students grasp lessons correctly the first time.
  • Promotes active participation — When communication is welcoming and clear, students ask questions and join discussions.
  • Models good language — Teacher's speech models correct vocabulary, pronunciation, and sentence structure for students.
  • Positive classroom climate — Courteous, consistent communication builds trust and reduces anxiety.
  • Assessment through feedback — By observing student responses, teacher can gauge understanding and adjust teaching.
  • Reduces classroom management issues — Clear instructions mean fewer misunderstandings and disruptions.
  • Non-verbal cues — Eye contact, gestures, and facial expressions maintain attention and convey enthusiasm and empathy.
Unit 11 · Very Important

Speech Defects

Appeared in 8/9 papers. Know types, causes, and teacher's role thoroughly.

What are Speech Defects?
Definition

Speech defects are abnormalities or disorders in the normal production of speech sounds that deviate significantly from accepted speech patterns, making communication difficult or unclear.

Types of Speech Defects
Stuttering / Stammering

Involuntary repetition or prolongation of sounds, syllables, or words. "I w-w-want water." Often linked to anxiety or neurological factors.

Lisping

Mispronunciation of sibilant sounds — "s" sounds like "th". "Sun" becomes "thun." Caused by incorrect tongue placement.

Cluttering

Speaking so rapidly that speech becomes unclear and disorganized. Words run together and are hard to follow.

Articulation Disorders

Inability to produce certain sounds correctly. Substituting, omitting, or distorting specific speech sounds.

Voice Disorders

Abnormalities in pitch, loudness, or quality of voice — hoarseness, unusual nasality, or extremely soft voice.

Mutism

Selective or total absence of speech. Often psychological — child can speak but refuses in certain situations.

Causes of Speech Defects

Physical Causes

  • Cleft palate or cleft lip
  • Tongue-tie (short frenulum)
  • Hearing impairment
  • Dental problems

Neurological Causes

  • Brain damage at birth
  • Cerebral palsy
  • Stroke or injury
  • Developmental delays

Psychological Causes

  • Anxiety, fear, trauma
  • Low self-confidence
  • Emotional distress
  • Selective mutism

Environmental Causes

  • Poor speech models at home
  • Lack of language stimulation
  • Bilingual/multilingual confusion
  • Imitation of someone with a speech defect
Role of Teacher in Correcting Speech Defects ★
1
Create a supportive environment

Never mock, mimic, or draw public attention to the child's speech difficulty. A safe, non-threatening classroom reduces anxiety — a major trigger for speech problems.

2
Give adequate time to speak

Do not rush or interrupt the child. Allow them to complete their speech at their own pace. Patience from the teacher signals acceptance to the whole class.

3
Model correct speech

Speak clearly and correctly. When the child makes an error, gently restate the correct form naturally in your response without directly correcting and embarrassing the child.

4
Use drama, songs, rhymes, role-play

These activities build confidence in speaking in a low-pressure, fun context. Choral speaking (whole class together) helps children with stuttering to speak more fluently.

5
Mirror and articulation exercises

For articulatory defects (like lisping), use mirror exercises where the child watches tongue and lip placement while speaking. Pronunciation drills done privately help.

6
Refer to speech therapist

For serious or persistent speech defects, the teacher should refer the child to a qualified speech-language therapist. The teacher's role is supportive, not to replace professional help.

7
Involve parents

Coordinate with parents to ensure consistent practice and support at home. Parents need guidance on what to do (be patient, model good speech) and what not to do (don't correct harshly).

Unit 12 · Important

Multilingualism

Appeared in 8/9 papers. Know definition, nature in Indian classrooms, and dialect/deficit theory.

Key Definitions

Multilingualism

The ability of an individual or community to use three or more languages for communication. India is predominantly multilingual.

Bilingualism

The ability to use two languages with competence. Common in border regions and urban areas where two language communities coexist.

Mother Tongue

The first language acquired naturally from birth, from parents and the home environment. It is the primary language through which a child understands the world.

School Dialect

The standard/prestige variety of language used officially in school. May differ from the student's home dialect — creates a gap that teachers must bridge.

Nature of Multilingualism in Indian Classrooms ★

India is one of the most linguistically diverse countries in the world, with over 122 major languages and more than 1,600 dialects. This diversity is directly reflected in classrooms.

  • Diverse linguistic backgrounds — In a single classroom, students may speak different mother tongues (Kannada, Tamil, Telugu, Urdu, Hindi, etc.).
  • Medium-home language mismatch — The medium of instruction (often English or state language) frequently differs from the child's home language, creating comprehension challenges.
  • Three Language Formula — Students learn (1) mother tongue, (2) regional/national language (Hindi), and (3) English — adding layers of linguistic complexity.
  • Home language as a resource — LAC encourages teachers to use the child's home language as a bridge, not treat it as an obstacle to learning.
  • Translanguaging — Students naturally move between languages (e.g., explaining something in Kannada, then switching to English for technical terms). This is a legitimate cognitive strategy, not a failure.
  • Code-switching — Shifting between languages or dialects in conversation depending on context, audience, and topic. Very common in Indian classrooms and should be respected.
Dialect and Deficit Theory ★ (2017 paper only)
Dialect

A dialect is a form of spoken language peculiar to a specific region, social group, or community. It may differ from the standard language in vocabulary, grammar, and pronunciation but is a complete and valid language system.

Deficit Theory (Eller, 1989) ★ 2017 Paper

Core claim: Children who speak non-standard dialects (regional dialects, working-class speech) are linguistically deprived — their language is a primitive, concrete form that is not an adequate vehicle for abstract thought and cognition.

Key points of the theory:

  • – Linguistically deprived child has a primitive language — not adequate vehicle for cognition
  • – Concreteness of dialect language makes it difficult to develop abstract thinking
  • – By age 6, children internalize the rules of their language (standard or non-standard)
  • – Linguistic styles deviate from standard — like educated vs. uneducated speech
  • – Dialect is deficient and deviates from standard English

Criticism: Linguists today largely reject the Deficit Theory. All dialects are rule-governed, complete language systems. The "deficit" reflects social prejudice, not linguistic reality. LAC takes a more positive view — multilingualism and dialect diversity are assets.
