Q.1
a) Explain the meaning and characteristics of gender stereotypes in the context of patriarchy. b) Explain the impact of family, society and culture in the development of gender stereotype roles.
4+6▾

a) Gender Stereotypes in the Context of Patriarchy

Gender stereotype is a generalised view or preconception about the attributes, characteristics or roles that men and women should possess. These are oversimplified and fixed ideas about how men and women are and ought to be.

Patriarchy is a social system in which men hold primary power and predominate in roles of political leadership, moral authority, social privilege, and control of property.

Characteristics of gender stereotypes:

  • Women are expected to be emotional, nurturing and domestically oriented
  • Men are expected to be rational, strong, breadwinners
  • Women are considered weak and dependent; men considered dominant and independent
  • Occupational stereotypes — nursing/teaching for women; engineering/politics for men
  • Stereotypes are socially constructed, not biologically determined
  • They limit individual potential and reinforce inequality

b) Impact of Family, Society and Culture on Gender Stereotype Roles

Family: First agent of socialisation. Children observe gender roles modelled by parents. Division of household duties teaches boys and girls their "expected roles." Sons given more freedom; daughters restricted.

Society: Society frames its own roles for different genders; insists members follow these roles; uses direct and indirect measures to control those who fail their role functions; helps in forming new trend-setting related to gender roles; motivates members following roles correctly.

Culture: Cultural norms, festivals, rituals and practices reinforce gender roles. Religious texts and traditions often prescribe different roles. Language and media carry cultural gender stereotypes. Dress codes, behaviour expectations — all culturally enforced.

Q.2
a) Mention the gender discriminations prevailing in different aspects of education. b) Mention the causes and measures to these gender discriminations.
6+4▾

a) Gender Discrimination in Different Aspects of Education

  • Enrolment: Lower enrolment of girls especially in rural/tribal areas; early marriage pulls girls out
  • Curriculum: Textbooks portray women in domestic roles; science/maths seen as "boys' subjects"
  • Teacher attitude: Teachers may unconsciously give more attention and encouragement to boys
  • Hidden curriculum: Unwritten rules and expectations that reinforce gender stereotypes in school
  • Facilities: Lack of separate toilets for girls; unsafe environment; no sanitary facilities
  • Vocational training: Girls steered towards domestic science; boys towards technical/mechanical
  • Higher education: Girls less likely to pursue science, technology, engineering and mathematics (STEM)

b) Causes and Measures

Causes: Patriarchal mindset; poverty and economic necessity; religious beliefs and customs; safety concerns for girls; parental attitude preferring son's education; lack of role models; gender-biased curriculum.

Measures: Introducing gender-based choice of streams; sexual harassment/abuse-free environment; proper sanitation for both genders; safety measures; gender neutral language; emphasis on life skill education; increasing awareness about universalisation of education; decreasing distance between school and home; free from partiality, scoldings and punishments.

Q.3
a) Contributions of Jyothiba Phule and Savithribai Phule in promotion of women education in India. b) Recommendations of NPE 1986, POA 1992 and NCF 2005 towards gender equality and empowerment of girls.
4+6▾

a) Contributions of Jyothiba Phule & Savithribai Phule

Jyothiba Phule: Founded India's first school for girls (1848, Pune); fought against caste and gender discrimination; established Satyashodhak Samaj to promote equal rights; influenced many women and responsible for their crusade against injustice and inequality; strongly opposed child marriage; fought against dowry system; encouraged simple marriage rituals; opened women's entry into national politics through non-violent methods; made women protest against injustice.

Savithribai Phule: First woman teacher in India; taught girls despite severe social opposition; worked tirelessly for girl child education; wrote poetry encouraging women to educate themselves; established shelters for widows and socially ostracised women; worked for abolition of child marriage; fought for the rights of Dalit women.

b) NPE 1986 Recommendations

Women instructors in non-formal education programmes have to receive special orientation to play an active role towards gender equality; emphasis on women's participation in vocational, technical and professional education; special programmes developed by research institutions and voluntary organisations to promote self-image and general awareness of women; active programmes to tackle women's problems by education institutes.

POA 1992

Preference in recruitment of teachers up to school level for women; cell to accelerate elimination of sexist bias and sex stereotypes from textbooks; women's cell of NCERT should take active help of all persons; teachers, trainers, planners and administrators sensitized to women's issues; women's cell in NCERT given responsibility for preparing the co-curriculum relating to women's equality.

NCF 2005

Support research to build perspective on relationship between gender and education; create banks of gender sensitive material; access to education for all girls; retention and quality of girls' education; establish gender as a critical marker of transformation and as an organizing principle; establish that gender is not a women's issue — it is peoples' issue; critical and proactive approach to equality and empowerment of girls.

a) Women Empowerment — Meaning and Indicators

Meaning: Women empowerment refers to the process of enabling women to have power and control over their own lives — social, political, economic and educational. It means giving women equal rights, opportunities and freedom to make their own choices.

Importance: To bring women to mainstream in par with men; to enhance national development by including neglected large population of women; to bring back the same good old status and positions women enjoyed during Vedic period; to put a full stop for all the sorrows suffered by women.

Indicators of Women Empowerment:

  • Qualitative indicators — women's levels of stabilization, attitudinal changes
  • Education and status of women
  • Employment — equal wages, treatment and facilities
  • Health and reproductive health, nutrition, age at marriage
  • Opportunity to decision making, economic self-sufficiency
  • Social mobility and political participation
  • Knowledge, legal rights, property rights, reproductive rights
  • Self-confidence, self-esteem, communication and leadership

b) Government Initiatives

  • Employment sphere: Equal Remuneration Act 1973; Unorganized Workers Social Security Act 2008; Maternity Benefit Act 1961; Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act 2013; Right to equality under Article 14

Q.5
a) Explain the concept of gender fair school environment. b) Discuss the role of teachers in promoting gender equality and empowerment of girls.
5+5▾

a) Gender Fair School Environment

A setting created by teachers, administrators and all stakeholders in which both male and female genders of students and staff feel the existence or get gender equality or equity in all the aspects of school systems.

vs Gender Mainstreaming: Gender mainstreaming is the public policy concept of assessing the different implications for men and women of any planned policy action including legislation and programmes in all areas and levels. It essentially offers a pluralistic approach that values the diversity among both men and women.

Features of gender fair school: Equal treatment of all students; gender-neutral language; curriculum free of stereotypes; equal participation in all activities; safe environment for all genders; policies against harassment; representation of women in school leadership.

b) Role of Teachers (from answer key)

  • Brainstorming, role play, games and debates through working in small mixed gender groups
  • Using gender neutral language
  • Using multiple strategies and interventions to ensure students have equal opportunities
  • Actively involving all girls and boys in dynamic teaching-learning process
  • Training students in co-scholastic activities with choices open
  • Establishing house system ensures healthy competitions and ample opportunities for students to work collaboratively
  • Motivating students to various programmes like cultural events, dance etc.
  • Students should be prepared for different Olympiads irrespective of their genders
  • Providing gender friendly guidance and counselling
  • Organizing exhibitions, debate competitions frequently on different topics for both genders

Q.8
How does gender equity differ from gender equality? As a teacher, what measures do you take to eliminate gender inequality?
2+3▾

Gender Equity vs Gender Equality

Gender equity means fairness of treatment for women and men according to their respective needs. This may include equal treatment or treatment that is different but considered equivalent in terms of rights, benefits, obligations and opportunities.

Gender equality entails the concept that all human beings, both men and women, are free to develop their personal abilities without limitations set by stereotypes, rigid gender roles and prejudices. Gender equality means that the different behaviour, aspirations and needs of women and men are considered, valued and favoured equally.

Teacher's Measures to Eliminate Gender Inequality

  • Providing examples of both genders achieving great heights in different fields
  • Encouraging and guiding both boys and girls in setting their life goals
  • Discovering activities where both boys and girls participate and understand each other's merits
  • Judicious use of criticisms and appreciations while handling different genders

Q.13

Sexual Harassment vs Sexual Abuse

Sexual Harassment (from answer key): Bullying or coercion of sexual nature; unwelcome or inappropriate promise of rewards for sexual favours; illegal and unethical; includes unwelcome sexual advances, request for sexual favours, verbal or physical harassment. Forms: unwelcome verbal/non-verbal messages, domestic violence, sexual bribery, assault, emotional threat, child sex abuse, pornography, rape, molestation, sexual determined behavior/body language.

Sexual Abuse: When someone in a position of power or authority takes advantage of a person's trust and respect to involve them in sexual activity. Can occur between child and adult, patient and doctor, student and teacher. Shock, fear, disbelief are immediate reactions; anxiety and PTSD are long-term symptoms.

Measures (Redressal from answer key)

Established redressal cell; gender sensitivity training; legal awareness; suggestion box; help desk; guidance and counselling; medical facilities; assertive behavior training; self-protection training; CCTV etc.

Q.1
a) What is gender inequality? b) List any eight causes for gender inequalities. c) Initiatives taken by government to promote women empowerment in legal and employment spheres.
2+4+4▾

a) Gender Inequality

Gender inequality is nothing but the absence of gender equality. It is an unequal treatment or perceptions of individuals wholly or partly due to their gender, arising from differences in socially constructed gender roles. It is a state of lacking equal ease of access to resources and opportunities including economic participation and decision making regardless of gender, and valuing different behaviours, aspirations and needs unequally regarding their gender. It is the idea and situation that men and women are not equal.

b) Eight Causes of Gender Inequality

  • Centuries of oppression of women in society
  • Existence of patriarchal society and patrilineal customs
  • Negative attitude of male gender towards female gender
  • Superiority complex and ego satisfaction of male gender embedded with selfishness
  • Emphasis on Androcentrism by culturally marginalizing Gynocentrism
  • Religious beliefs, bias, prejudice, superstitions
  • Double standards regarding attitude towards women
  • Manu Dharma Sastra's boundaries for women; preference for son; dowry system; male chauvinism; impact of media

c) Government Initiatives — Legal Sphere

Indian Penal Code: Rape (Sec 376 IPC); Molestation (Sec 354 IPC); Sexual harassment (Sec 509 IPC). Special laws: Dowry Prohibition Act 1961; SITA 1956; Domestic Violence Act 2005; Family Courts Act 1954; Maternity Benefit Act 1961; MTP Act; Equal Remuneration Act 1976.

Employment Domain: Equal Remuneration Act 1973; Unorganized Workers Social Security Act 2008; Maternity Benefit Act 1961; Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act 2013; Right to equality under Article 14.

Q.1
a) Explain briefly the concept of sex and gender. b) What is gender discrimination? c) Explain gender discrimination in different spheres of Indian women's life.
3+2+5▾

a) Sex and Gender

Sex refers to the biological and physiological characteristics that define men and women — chromosomes, hormonal profiles, internal and external sex organs. It is determined at birth and is binary (male/female).

Gender refers to the socially constructed roles, behaviours, activities and attributes that a given society considers appropriate for men and women. Gender is learned and can vary across cultures and over time.

b) Gender Discrimination

Gender discrimination is the unequal or unfavourable treatment of individuals based on their gender. It involves treating people differently — usually women — in a way that is unjust or prejudicial, based on stereotyped gender roles rather than individual merit.

c) Spheres of Gender Discrimination in Indian Women's Life

  • Education: Lower enrolment, early dropout, gender-biased curriculum, less encouragement for girls in STEM
  • Employment: Wage gap, glass ceiling, sexual harassment at workplace, fewer leadership roles
  • Health: Sex-selective abortion, female foeticide, neglect of girl child's health
  • Political: Low representation, barriers to political participation
  • Family: Dowry, domestic violence, unequal household responsibilities, restriction of movement
  • Religion: No entry to certain religious places; restrictions on religious participation

a & b) Transgender — Meaning and Concept

Transgenders are individuals who do not conform to the gender role expected of their biological sex. Transgender people are individuals with the biological characteristics of one sex, who identify themselves as the opposite gender and some types of surgical alterations or hormonal treatments that change their physical appearance. They express clothes, facial and body expression, language, voice and behaviour as the opposite sex. They are getting bullied, raped, beaten up, murdered and are committing suicide. The numbers are staggering — these are the reality of what it is like to be trans today.

Q.4
a) What is women empowerment? b) Explain briefly the need of women empowerment. c) Explain the measures undertaken by Govt. of India towards women empowerment.
2+3+5▾

a) Women Empowerment

The process of enabling women to have power and control over their own lives — social, political, economic and educational. It means giving women equal rights, opportunities and freedom to make their own choices.

b) Need for Women Empowerment

  • To bring women to mainstream in par with men who are deprived of human rights for a very long time
  • To enhance national development by including the neglected large population of talented, capable and resourceful women
  • To bring back the same good old status and positions women enjoyed during Vedic period
  • To put a full stop for all the sorrows suffered by women

c) Government of India Measures

  • Legal: Dowry Prohibition Act; Domestic Violence Act; Equal Remuneration Act; Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act 2013
  • Education: Mahila Samakhya; Kasturba Gandhi Balika Vidyalaya; Beti Bachao Beti Padhao; scholarships for girls
  • Employment: Maternity Benefit Act; MGNREGS ensuring women's employment; equal pay legislation
  • Health: Janani Suraksha Yojana; free healthcare for women; Pradhan Mantri Matru Vandana Yojana
  • Political: 33% reservation in Panchayat; 73rd and 74th Amendment
  • Economic: Women Self-Help Groups; Mudra loans for women entrepreneurs

a) What is sexual harassment? b) Explain the constitutional measures to address sexual harassment. c) Explain briefly the school in tackling sexual harassment.

a) Sexual Harassment

Bullying or coercion of sexual nature; unwelcome or inappropriate promise of rewards for sexual favours. Sexual harassment is illegal; unethical; unlawful to harass a person because of their sex. b) Constitutional/Legal Measures

  • Article 14 — Right to equality; Article 15 — no discrimination on basis of sex
  • Article 21 — Right to life with dignity
  • Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace (Prevention, Prohibition and Redressal) Act 2013
  • IPC Sections 354, 376, 509 deal with molestation, rape and sexual harassment
  • POCSO Act 2012 — Protection of Children from Sexual Offences
  • Vishaka Guidelines 1997 — landmark Supreme Court guidelines on workplace sexual harassment

c) School's Role in Tackling Sexual Harassment

  • Establish a redressal cell / Internal Complaints Committee
  • Gender sensitivity training for all staff and students
  • Clear policies against harassment with defined consequences
  • Safe and secure physical environment — proper lighting, CCTV
  • Guidance and counselling support for victims
  • Awareness programs about what constitutes harassment
  • Teach assertive behaviour and self-protection skills

c) Patriarchy, Masculinity and Feminism

Patriarchy: A social system in which men hold primary power and predominate in roles of political leadership, moral authority, social privilege and control of property. Men are dominant over women and children in family and society.

Masculinity: Qualities or attributes regarded as characteristic of men. Socially constructed attributes including strength, dominance, independence, emotional stoicism. Hegemonic masculinity is the dominant form that legitimizes men's dominant position in society.

Feminism: A range of social, political and economic movements and ideologies that share a common goal — to define, establish and achieve political, economic, personal and social equality for women. Challenges patriarchy and advocates for women's rights and equal opportunities.

b) Four Factors Responsible

c) Gender Inequality vs Gender Equality

Gender inequality refers to unequal treatment or perceptions of individuals based on gender — it is the problem.
