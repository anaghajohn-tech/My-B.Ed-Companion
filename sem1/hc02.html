Q.1
a) What is Education? b) Differentiate between narrow and broader meaning of education. c) Discuss the general aims of Education with respect to Contemporary Society.
(2+3+5 = 10 marks)
▼
a) What is Education? (2 marks)
Education is a lifelong process of developing the individual's physical, mental, social, and spiritual faculties. It begins when the child enters the world and continues until death. It is the systematic development of knowledge, skills, values, and attitudes.
b) Narrow vs Broader Meaning (3 marks)
Narrow MeaningBroader Meaning
Planned, organised, deliberateNon-planned, life-long, informal
Equivalent to schoolingEquivalent to life itself
Fixed entry and exitTakes place in all stages of life
Fixed aims, curriculum, evaluationComplete experiences, knowledge-oriented
Formal means and endsInformal means and ends
Students are homogeneous groupBelongs to all ages
c) General Aims of Education in Contemporary Society (5 marks)
  • Vocational aim — prepare students for earning a livelihood
  • Development of culture — preserve and promote cultural heritage
  • Quality of life — improve living standards
  • Health — develop physically and mentally healthy citizens
  • Economic development — contribute to national progress
  • Increasing production — train skilled workforce
  • Democratic values — develop democratic citizenship
  • Emotional integration — foster national unity
  • Human values — develop ethical and moral character
  • National values, International understanding, Gender equality, promotion of patriotism
Q.2
a) What is social change? b) How does education become an instrument of social change? c) What is the role of teacher in bringing social change among students?
(2+4+4 = 10 marks)
▼
a) Meaning of Social Change (2 marks)
Social change refers to alterations in the structure and functioning of society over time — changes in values, norms, institutions, relationships, and ways of life. It is universal and continuous.
b) Education as Instrument of Social Change (4 marks)
  • Changes outlook and attitudes of people
  • Imparts knowledge, skill, and social values
  • Works as progress and conservation of social change
  • Brings radical improvement in economic development
  • Develops scientific and social attitudes
  • Increases political awareness which drives social change
  • Changes the pattern of social relations
  • Promotes national and social values
c) Role of Teacher in Social Change (4 marks)
  • Teacher is an agent — imparts social education
  • Trains students to become good citizens
  • Works as coordinator between education and society
  • Organises social service programmes
  • Conducts vocational classes
  • Works for mass literacy upliftment of women
  • Promotes communal harmony
  • Uses community resources for learning
Q.3
a) How are 'Education' and 'Philosophy' related to each other? b) What are the educational contributions of J.J. Rousseau?
(5+5 = 10 marks)
▼
a) Relationship between Education and Philosophy (5 marks)
  • Philosophy is the foundation of education — education is the practical activity
  • Philosophy is the source of knowledge — education applies knowledge to real life
  • Education is the laboratory in which all philosophical thoughts are tested
  • Philosophy supplies values — education inculcates those values
  • Philosophy solves the problems of education
  • Great philosophers are also great educationists
  • Philosophy is the soul — education is the body; philosophy is contemplative, education is dynamic
  • They are two sides of the same coin
b) Educational Contributions of J.J. Rousseau (5 marks)
  • Meaning of Education: Book "Emile" — 3 sources of education: nature, man, and things
  • Negative Education: Do not impose adult values on children
  • Aims: Natural development, stages — physical development, sense training, intellectual development
  • Education for manhood through nature
  • Curriculum: Play activities, health activities, physical exercises, sensory education, language, geography, mathematics, nature study, moral education, history, music, culture, family life, sex education
  • Child-centred education — respect for child's nature

▼
a) Meaning of Socialisation (2 marks)
Socialisation is the process where the original nature of a person is transferred into human nature — the process by which an individual becomes a social being, learning the values, norms, and roles of society.
b) Curricular and Co-curricular Activities for Socialisation (8 marks)

  • Common uniform, NSS Activities, NCC
  • Swach Bharat programmes, exhibitions and film shows
Q.5
a) What are the purposes of 'Questioning' as a device of teaching? b) What are the characteristics of good questions? c) Write two critical thinking questions on any topic.
(4+4+2 = 10 marks)
▼
a) Purposes of Questioning (4 marks)
  • To know what the child already knows
  • Keep students alert and attentive
  • Know whether students are following the lesson
  • Revise the lesson and fix knowledge
  • Directs and encourages thought; stimulates curiosity
  • Tests amount of learning
  • Knows the gap in learning
  • Finds reason for failure of learning
b) Characteristics of Good Questions (4 marks)
  • Simple, clear, specific and precise
  • Concise — not too lengthy
  • According to age, level and ability of the child
  • Direct and purposeful
  • Thought-provoking
  • Sequential — relevant to the topic
  • Free from vagueness
  • Should not contain too many ideas in a single question
c) Two Critical Thinking Questions (2 marks)
  • "If education were available to all equally, how would Indian society be different today?"
  • "Should technology replace traditional classroom teaching? Justify with reasons."

Naturalism (explanation)

  • Curriculum: Natural sciences, physical activities, sensory training

Q.16
a) What is project based teaching? b) What is the importance of project based teaching? (2+3 = 5 marks)
▼
Definition
Project-based teaching is a dynamic classroom approach in which students actively explore real-world challenges and acquire deeper knowledge — students not only gain knowledge but also gain skills by working.
Importance
  • Students acquire deeper knowledge through active exploration of real-world challenges
  • PBL integrates knowing and doing
  • Students learn knowledge and elements of the core curriculum but also apply what they know to solve authentic problems
  • Helps greater depth of understanding of concepts, broader knowledge base
  • Improved communication and interpersonal skills
  • Enhanced leadership skills, increased creativity, improved writing skills
  • Students develop confidence and self-direction
  • Assessment of project-based work is more meaningful to them

a) What is culture? b) How can education help in preserving the culture of a society? (2+3 = 5 marks)

Meaning of Culture
Culture is the total pattern of human behaviour — the sum total of a society's values, beliefs, customs, traditions, art, language, and achievements passed from generation to generation.
Education in Preservation of Culture
  • School and teacher are active agencies of cultural development
  • School can spread and preserve culture by conducting cultural camps, literacy activities, programmes
  • Inter-cultural competitions, cultural exchange programmes
  • Teaching folk culture, exhibitions on cultural heritage
  • Collection of books on Indian culture
  • Excursions — visit important cultural monuments
  • Crafts, dance, music; conducting rallies etc.

Q.13
What are the characteristics of Gandhiji's concept of Basic Education? State the reasons for its failure. (5 marks)
▼
Characteristics of Basic Education (Nai Talim)
  • Free and compulsory education for 7 years
  • Mother tongue as medium of instruction
  • Craft-centred — learning through productive work (spinning, weaving, agriculture)
  • Self-supporting — education to pay for itself through craft production
  • Integrated curriculum — all subjects taught through the craft
  • Moral and spiritual development
  • Develop the whole personality — head, heart, and hand
Reasons for Failure
  • Teachers were not trained in craft-based teaching
  • Self-supporting concept was impractical
  • Craft products had no market value
  • Parents preferred academic education for white-collar jobs
  • Lack of funds and government support
Q.14
Explain briefly the educational contributions of John Dewey. (5 marks)
▼
  • Philosophy: Pragmatism — "learning by doing"; truth is what works
  • Aim: Growth itself is the aim — "Education is not preparation for life; education is life itself"
  • Child-centred education: Child's interests and experience are the starting point
  • Curriculum: Activity-based; connected to real life and social environment
  • Method: Project method, problem-solving, experimentation
  • School as society: School is a miniature democratic society
  • Democracy and Education: His famous work — education must prepare citizens for democracy
  • Emphasis on social experience and cooperative learning

Q.7
Explain the salient features of Idealism. (5 marks)
▼

Q.10
"Education is both process and product." Analyse this statement. (5 marks)
▼
Education as Process
  • Education is a continuous, lifelong activity — never ends
  • It is a dynamic interaction between teacher, student, and environment
  • Involves teaching, learning, experiencing, reflecting
  • The journey of learning is itself valuable — not just the destination
Education as Product
  • Results in measurable outcomes — knowledge, skills, values, attitudes
  • Certificates, degrees, competencies are the products
  • Behavioural changes in the individual are the product
  • Contributes to national development — educated citizenry is the product
Both aspects are inseparable — you cannot have a meaningful product without a sound process, and the process is given direction by the desired product.
